# Why TxDOT Highway Construction Is So Slow: Structural Mechanisms

**Research compiled from TxDOT official documents, federal agency reports, academic studies, and news sources.**

## 1. TxDOT Project Approval Pipeline: Concept to Construction

### The Six Phases

TxDOT's official project development process has six major phases, all of which must be completed before a shovel hits the ground. ([TxDOT Project Development Process Manual](https://www.txdot.gov/manuals/des/pdp/pdp.pdf))

| Phase | Description | Typical Duration |
|---|---|---|
| **Planning** | Identifies need via safety data, congestion metrics, long-range plans; produces Unified Transportation Program (UTP) | 5–20 years in long-range planning cycles |
| **Programming** | Project added to UTP; authorization to spend funds on development activities | 1–3 years |
| **Preliminary Engineering** | Conceptual design, environmental review (NEPA), public involvement, right-of-way mapping | 3–10+ years |
| **Final Design** | Full engineering plans, specs, bid documents | 1–3 years |
| **Letting** | Competitive bid solicitation, contractor selection | 6–18 months |
| **Construction** | Actual building | 2–10 years depending on project scope |

**Total elapsed time from initial study to construction: typically 9–19 years for major highway projects.** The FHWA has explicitly stated this range. ([FHWA, via Alaska DOT NEPA study](https://dot.alaska.gov/stwddes/desenviron/assets/pdf/resources/nepareviewtime.pdf))

A Montana DOT brochure states plainly: "It can take 8 years to complete a road project, but long-range planning starts some 20 years prior to project start." ([Montana DOT](http://www.mdt.mt.gov/pubinvolve/jackrabbit/docs/How-a-Road-Gets-Built.pdf))

### Key Bottlenecks Within Each Phase

**Environmental Review (NEPA) — The Single Largest Time Sink**

Every federally-funded highway project requires compliance with the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA, 1970). There are three tiers:
- **Categorical Exclusion (CE)**: Minor projects with no significant impact. Weeks to months.
- **Environmental Assessment (EA)**: Moderate projects. Average **9.6 months** (2021–2023), but can stretch much longer with public challenges. ([CEQ EIS Timeline Report 2025](https://ceq.doe.gov/docs/nepa-practice/CEQ_EIS_Timeline_Report_2025-1-13.pdf))
- **Environmental Impact Statement (EIS)**: Full review for major projects.

For EIS — the type required for major highway expansions:
- **Median completion time (NOI to Final EIS): 2.8 years** (2019–2024 federal average)
- **FHWA highway projects historically: 6.7–7.37 year median** — nearly double the government-wide average. ([R Street Institute NEPA analysis](https://www.rstreet.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/FINAL_RSTREET234.pdf))
- In the 1970s, FHWA NEPA compliance averaged 2.2 years. By 2016, it had risen to 5.2 years. ([R Street Institute](https://www.rstreet.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/FINAL_RSTREET234.pdf))
- Approximately 25% of EIS reviews take more than 6 years; 9.5% take 10+ years.
- The final FHWA EIS document averages **742 pages** in length. ([Trump White House CEQ summary](https://trumpwhitehouse.archives.gov/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/20200819-FINAL-Summary-of-NEPA-Rule.pdf))

Texas gained "NEPA Assignment" authority from FHWA in 2014, meaning TxDOT handles its own environmental certifications rather than waiting for federal agency review. This eliminated one layer of review but did not eliminate the substantive requirements. ([AASHTO Center for Environmental Excellence](https://environment.transportation.org/case_study/txdot-accrues-multiple-benefits-from-nepa-assignment-responsibilities/))

**Right-of-Way Acquisition — Seven Sub-Processes**

ROW acquisition at TxDOT involves seven distinct sequential/overlapping processes: ([TxDOT ROW Manual](https://www.txdot.gov/manuals/des/pdp/chapter-6--right-of-way-and-utilities/6-3-row-acquisition-process.html))

1. Property description and ROW mapping
2. Title search and curative procedures (clearing liens, ownership issues)
3. Appraisal
4. Negotiation (owner has minimum 30 days to review initial offer; final offer cannot be sent fewer than 30 days after initial offer)
5. Eminent domain / condemnation (when negotiation fails — triggers court proceedings)
6. Relocation of displaced persons/businesses
7. Utility adjustment (moving underground/overhead utilities out of new ROW)

Key rule: ROW clearance certification must be complete before bid advertisement. Delays in ROW acquisition after a construction contract is let result in contractor delay claims and change orders at TxDOT's expense.

TxDOT's annual ROW budget is approximately $475 million statewide. ([Texas County Progress](https://countyprogress.com/right-of-way-acquisition-at-txdot/))

**Public Comment Periods**

Major projects require multiple public involvement rounds:
- Scoping meetings during EIS initiation
- Draft EIS public comment period (minimum 45–60 days under federal rules)
- Final EIS / Record of Decision
- Additional hearings for design changes

Each comment period can extend 6 weeks to several months. On contested projects, advocacy groups can extend timelines by filing lawsuits after each decision point.

**Programming / UTP Cycle**

TxDOT's Unified Transportation Program (UTP) is a 10-year planning document updated annually. Projects must be listed in the UTP before development authorization. The UTP process itself involves:
- Annual development cycle with Texas Transportation Commission approval
- Coordination with 25 TxDOT Districts and metropolitan planning organizations
- Federal Statewide Transportation Improvement Program (STIP) incorporation

Projects can sit in the UTP for years awaiting sufficient funding commitment before moving to the next phase.

## 2. Specific Examples: Texas Project Delays and Cost Overruns

### I-35 Capital Express Central (Austin) — 35+ Years and Counting

**The core problem: a project that began planning in the late 1980s is still under construction today.**

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| Late 1980s–2004 | TxDOT I-35 Major Investment Study (Georgetown to Buda). Proposed HOV lanes, additional lanes. Completed 2004. None of the improvements advanced due to funding and community opposition. |
| 2008 | Texas Transportation Commission creates I-35 Corridor Advisory Committee |
| 2010 | City of Austin funds $1.5M feasibility study for downtown I-35 improvements |
| 2011 | Texas Legislature passes Rider 42 prioritizing congested corridor studies; Mobility35 Program launched |
| 2012 | TxDOT launches Planning and Environmental Linkages (PEL) study |
| 2013–2014 | Downtown Stakeholder Working Group formed; two alternatives evaluated; Reconnect Austin alternative deemed unfeasible |
| 2014–2015 | Five neighborhood workshops; aesthetic working group launched |
| 2016–2017 | Open houses for North, Central, and South project variants |
| 2019 | Texas Transportation Commission approves funding for North and South projects |
| 2020 | Commission approves $3.4B for Central project; EIS launched with three alternatives; project fully funded at $4.9B |
| Nov 2020 – Sept 2021 | Three rounds of virtual public scoping meetings for Central EIS (extended due to community pressure from 30 to 45 days each round) |
| Jan 2022 | TxDOT releases Modified Alternative 3; presents at public VOICE meeting |
| Jan 2023 | Draft EIS (DEIS) released — **7,000 pages including 23 appendices** |
| Jan–Mar 2023 | 60-day public comment period on DEIS |
| Aug 2023 | Final EIS and Record of Decision issued |
| May & Sept 2022 | South and North projects let for construction bids |
| Nov 2022 | South project breaks ground |
| Mar 2023 | North project breaks ground |
| Oct 2024 | **Central project breaks ground** — the 8-mile downtown section |
| 2027 (est.) | Phase 1 (bridge/frontage roads) to begin on Central project |
| 2029 (est.) | Phase 2 (caps/main expansion) to begin |
| 2032–2033 (est.) | Anticipated completion |

**From first planning study to construction start: approximately 35 years.**

**Cost escalation:**
- Original Central project estimate: $4.5–4.9 billion
- Cap and Stitch overlay (City of Austin's add-on): initially estimated at $868 million, rose to $1.4 billion by late 2024 — **a 61% increase in one year** ([Austin Monitor](https://austinmonitor.com/stories/2024/11/i-35-caps-plan-reconsidered-as-cost-estimates-soar-by-61/))
- Cap design costs alone rose from $19M (May 2024) to $25M (December 2024) — **32% in 7 months** ([Austin Chronicle](https://www.austinchronicle.com/news/i-35s-cap-and-stitch-is-getting-messy/))
- The Central project (Phase 2) was pushed back three years in late 2025, with TxDOT simultaneously accelerating the City of Austin's funding commitment deadline from November 2026 to May 2026 — while the design was still only 30% complete. ([KXAN](https://www.kxan.com/traffic/traffic-projects/i-35-expansion-project/txdot-delays-i-35-expansion-project-roadway-element-construction-moves-up-funding-timeline-for-austin/))
- The project's 8-mile Central stretch alone costs $4.5B, or roughly **$562 million per mile**.

### MoPac (Loop 1) South — 13+ Years of Environmental Review with No Construction

MoPac South was first included in Austin's city plan in **1961**. The corridor has been studied repeatedly for decades.

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| 1961 | MoPac included in City of Austin Master Plan |
| 1973–1991 | MoPac built in segments from downtown to SH 45 |
| 1994 | TxDOT first proposes managed lanes on portions of Loop 1 |
| 2010 | CTRMA relaunches managed lanes study |
| 2012 | Environmental study completed; Finding of No Significant Impact for MoPac North (Cesar Chavez to Parmer Lane) |
| 2013 | MoPac Improvement Project (North) construction begins; MoPac South environmental study initiated (Cesar Chavez to Slaughter Lane) |
| 2015 | North section express lanes originally scheduled to open (September) |
| 2016 | North section still not open; MoPac South put on hold due to Save Our Springs lawsuit alleging NEPA non-compliance |
| Oct 2016 | First MoPac North express lane section opens — more than a year behind schedule |
| July 2018 | MoPac South lawsuit settled in CTRMA's favor by 5th Circuit |
| 2013–2026 | MoPac South environmental study ongoing — **13+ years** |
| Feb 2026 | Draft Environmental Assessment for MoPac South finally released; public comment period March–May 2026 |
| ~2030 (est.) | Earliest possible construction start if EA approved without further legal challenge |

The project proposes adding **8 miles** of express lanes along MoPac South. The environmental study alone has consumed over a decade. ([TxDOT public hearing notice](https://www.txdot.gov/projects/hearings-meetings/austin/2026/mopac-loop1-expressway-ea-030926.html); [Wikipedia Loop 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Texas_State_Highway_Loop_1))

### SH-130 Segments 5–6 — Traffic Projection Failure Leads to Bankruptcy

SH-130 is a 91-mile toll road east of Austin designed to relieve I-35 congestion. The project illustrates a different failure mode: a project built relatively quickly under a P3 arrangement that then collapsed financially due to wildly optimistic traffic forecasts.

| Timeline | Event |
|---|---|
| 2007 | TxDOT signs Comprehensive Development Agreement with SH 130 Concession Company (Cintra/Zachry) for 50-year P3 concession |
| 2008 | Financial close; $430M TIFIA federal loan signed |
| April 2009 | Construction begins |
| Oct/Nov 2012 | Road opens to traffic — 3.5 years construction for 40 miles |
| Sept 2012 | Speed limit set at 85 mph (highest in US), triggering $100M additional concession fee |
| 2013–2015 | Traffic far below projections; revenue insufficient to cover $1.3B debt service |
| March 2016 | SH 130 Concession Company files Chapter 11 bankruptcy |
| Sept 2016 | Cintra relinquishes ownership to creditors |
| July 2017 | Road exits bankruptcy under new management (Strategic Value Partners); $260M in new debt issued |
| 2062 | Concession contract expiration — new owners collect tolls until then on road with nearly no remaining debt |

**The original $1.3B project was funded entirely privately (plus $430M federal TIFIA loan), opened on time, but the traffic/revenue model was catastrophically wrong.** Taxpayers also subsidize truck tolls at $18.7M/year to attract freight traffic that never materialized organically. ([Texas Tribune](https://www.texastribune.org/2016/03/02/sh-130-toll-road-files-bankruptcy/); [Austin County News Online](https://austincountynewsonline.com/texans-angered-sh-130-bankruptcy-deal-wipes-money-owed-taxpayers/); [FHWA Project Profile](https://www.fhwa.dot.gov/ipd/project_profiles/tx_sh130.aspx))

## 3. Federal Requirements That Add Time

### NEPA Environmental Review

As detailed above, NEPA is the dominant time-adding requirement. Key facts:

- **Established**: 1970 (same year as California's more stringent CEQA)
- **Highway EIS median timeline (FHWA)**: 6.7–7.37 years historically; the federal government-wide median is 3.5 years — highway projects take roughly **double** the average agency. ([R Street Institute](https://www.rstreet.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/FINAL_RSTREET234.pdf))
- **The 1970–1973 inflection**: Real spending per mile on US Interstate construction increased more than three-fold from the 1960s to the 1980s, driven primarily by post-NEPA litigation and community challenge opportunities. The relationship between local wealth and project expense quintupled in statistical significance after 1970. ([Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/why-does-building-and-maintaining-highways-in-the-us-cost-so-much/); [Reason](https://reason.com/2021/03/25/why-does-american-infrastructure-cost-more-and-take-longer-to-build-than-it-used-to/))
- **Litigation risk**: After NEPA and the 1971 Supreme Court decision in *Citizens to Preserve Overton Park v. Volpe* (which curtailed judicial deference to agency decisions), any citizen or group can challenge highway project approvals in federal court. Rethink35 filed a lawsuit over I-35 Central; MoPac South was halted for 2+ years by a Save Our Springs suit.

**Statutory reform context**: The Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023 set a statutory 2-year deadline for EIS completion and 1-year for EAs. These are aspirational caps that have not yet been fully implemented. The CEQ's 2025 data shows the median is now approaching 2.2 years for all agencies, but FHWA highway projects have historically run far longer. ([CEQ EIS Timeline Report 2025](https://ceq.doe.gov/docs/nepa-practice/CEQ_EIS_Timeline_Report_2025-1-13.pdf))

### Davis-Bacon Act Prevailing Wage Requirements

- **Enacted**: 1931 (has never been repealed)
- **Applies to**: All federally funded construction projects over $2,000
- **Mechanism**: Requires contractors to pay workers the "prevailing wage" — in practice, typically set equal to local union wage rates
- **Cost impact on highway construction**:
  - Davis-Bacon wages average **22% higher** than market rates (Joint Economic Committee, 2011)
  - For highway construction specifically, the average Davis-Bacon wage was **34% higher** than BLS-reported average wages in sampled counties ([JEC Senate, 2011](https://www.jec.senate.gov/public/_cache/files/1ac1b544-b9ce-447d-bade-f88f5dafa4b2/dba-increases-cost-decreases-employment-03-31-2011.pdf))
  - Heritage Foundation estimates: inflates average highway construction costs by **5–38%** depending on state
  - Mackinac Center 2023 study: prevailing wage laws increase road construction costs by **8.5–14.3%** ([Mackinac Center](https://www.mackinac.org/S2023-04))
  - Federal construction costs run **150–200% higher** than comparable private construction, with Davis-Bacon as one key factor
  - Additional compliance burden: extensive payroll reporting, certification requirements, audit exposure — creating paperwork delays independent of wage levels

### Buy America / Build America Buy America (BABA)

- **Original Buy America**: Applied to iron and steel in FHWA projects since 1983
- **Build America Buy America Act (BABA)**: Enacted November 2021 as part of Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act — the most expansive domestic preference law in US history
- **Scope**: Now covers iron/steel, manufactured products, AND construction materials for all federally funded infrastructure
- **Construction materials newly covered**: Non-ferrous metals, plastics, glass, fiber optic cable, lumber, engineered wood, drywall
- **Manufactured products waiver**: The longstanding general waiver (in place since 1983) was rescinded effective January 2025, with phase 2 taking effect October 2026 — requiring 55% domestic content for all manufactured products
- **Procurement impact**: Contractors must now source or certify domestic origin for a vastly expanded list of materials. Project-specific waivers require separate federal approval processes, adding administrative time. Supply chain constraints from BABA requirements can delay material procurement. ([FHWA Buy America page](https://www.fhwa.dot.gov/construction/cqit/buyam.cfm); [Venable BABA update](https://www.venable.com/insights/publications/2025/09/build-america-buy-america-act-update))

### Section 4(f) of the Highway Act of 1966

Not always mentioned but equally important: Section 4(f) prohibits use of publicly owned parks, recreation areas, wildlife refuges, or historic sites for federally funded highway projects unless there is "no feasible and prudent alternative." This creates additional analysis requirements and litigation exposure on any project near parks. MoPac South's 2026 environmental assessment explicitly invokes Section 4(f) for temporary construction easements on adjacent park land.

## 4. TxDOT Funding: Gas Tax Stagnation and the Prop 1/Prop 7 Patch

### The Gas Tax Problem

**Texas state gas tax**: 20 cents per gallon (gasoline and diesel)
**Last raised**: **1991** — has not changed in 34 years ([Every Texan](https://everytexan.org/2015/01/13/where-does-the-gasoline-tax-go/))

**Federal gas tax**: 18.4 cents per gallon (gasoline), 24.4 cents (diesel)
**Last raised**: **1993** — has not changed in 32 years ([TxDOT Funding Brochure 2022](https://ftp.txdot.gov/pub/txdot-info/fin/funding-brochure-2022.pdf))

**The purchasing power problem**: Both state and federal gas taxes are set in nominal cents-per-gallon terms, not indexed to inflation. The Highway Trust Fund has required general revenue transfers from Congress since **2008** because gas tax revenues no longer cover costs. Meanwhile:
- Vehicles are dramatically more fuel-efficient (less gas purchased per mile driven)
- EVs pay $0 in gas taxes (though Texas has since added a fee)
- Construction costs have risen dramatically — Interstate construction costs increased **more than 4x in real terms** from the late 1950s to the early 1990s ([American Economic Association research](https://www.aeaweb.org/research/charts/infrastructure-costs-highways-us))

Federal funds account for **over 30%** of TxDOT's budget. Over one-third of TxDOT's spending depends on a tax rate last set in 1993.

### Proposition 1 (2014)

- **Approved**: November 2014 by 80% of Texas voters
- **Mechanism**: Constitutional amendment redirecting a portion of oil and natural gas production (severance) taxes to the State Highway Fund
- **Specifically**: 75% of annual oil/gas production tax revenue above a 1987 baseline threshold — after ensuring the Economic Stabilization Fund (Rainy Day Fund) doesn't fall below $7.5B
- **First deposit**: FY 2015
- **Volatility problem**: Oil and gas revenue is highly cyclical, making Prop 1 an unreliable planning base. In years of low oil prices, deposits can be minimal.
- **Expiration**: Transfers expire after FY 2035 (December 31, 2034) unless extended by the Legislature. The 88th Legislature (2023) extended it to December 31, 2042.
- **Scale**: Estimated $875M/year on historical average ([TxDOT Funding Brochure FY2026-2027](https://www.txdot.gov/content/dam/docs/division/fin/texas-department-transportation-funding-2027.pdf))

### Proposition 7 (2015)

- **Approved**: November 2015 by 83% of Texas voters
- **Mechanism**: Constitutional amendment dedicating portions of state sales/use tax and motor vehicle sales/rental tax to State Highway Fund
- **Component 1**: $2.5B/year of state sales and use tax revenue that exceeds the first $28B collected annually (effective FY 2018; expires FY 2042)
- **Component 2**: 35% of motor vehicle sales/rental tax revenue above $5B annually (effective FY 2020; expires FY 2039)
- **Restriction**: Funds may ONLY be used for non-toll public roadways or to repay Prop 12 general obligation bonds — cannot fund toll roads
- **Scale**: Has deposited $22.7 billion total to the State Highway Fund since 2016; FY 2025 deposit was $3.21 billion ([TxDOT Prop 7 page](https://www.txdot.gov/about/financial-management/funding-needs-sources/proposition-7-funding.html))
- **Legislative override**: Legislature can reduce deposits by up to 50% with a 2/3 vote — this was controversial in 2017 when budget writers considered clawing back funds

### Overall TxDOT Budget Picture

TxDOT's FY2026–2027 biennial budget: **~$39.92 billion** total appropriations

Funding source breakdown (approximate):
- Federal funds: ~$11.3B (30%+ of budget)
- Prop 7 funds: ~$7.85B
- State Highway Fund (traditional — gas tax, registration fees): ~$8.73B
- Prop 1 funds: ~$3.52B
- Other (General Revenue, bonds, tolls): remainder

**The core tension**: Traditional gas tax revenue (the reliable, flexible source) is structurally declining in real purchasing power. Prop 1 and Prop 7 were added as workarounds to avoid raising the gas tax. But Prop 7 funds are restricted to non-toll roads, and Prop 1 is volatile and temporary. The result is that TxDOT's largest projects — particularly urban mega-projects like I-35 Central — require complex funding assemblages (state discretionary funds, CAMPO commitments, federal TIFIA loans, etc.) that add coordination time and conditionality.

## 5. International Comparison: How Fast Do Other Countries Build?

### China vs. United States: The Freeway Gap

| Metric | China | United States |
|---|---|---|
| Year of first freeway | 1998 | ~1940 (parkways); Interstate system 1956 |
| Total freeway miles (2019) | 93,000 | 67,000 |
| Annual new freeway miles added | ~4,000–5,000/year | <800/year |
| Highway cost per mile | Lower (partially due to lower labor costs and land acquisition processes) | 3x higher than comparable countries |

China went from **zero** freeways in 1997 to surpassing the entire US Interstate network in total miles by **2014** — in just 17 years. The US Interstate system took 35 years (1956–1991) to complete its primary buildout. ([Cato Institute](https://www.cato.org/commentary/closing-china-us-transportation-gap); [Thoreau Institute](https://ti.org/antiplanner/?p=17975))

**Structural explanations for China's speed:**
- State land ownership: No private property rights to negotiate. The Chinese government can appropriate land and begin construction almost immediately.
- No NEPA equivalent with litigation rights for citizens
- Continuous multi-year funding commitments (5-year plans) not subject to political turnover
- Engineers (not lawyers) dominate government decision-making — what one analyst calls an "engineering state" vs. a "lawyerly society" ([Vox/Dan Wang analysis](https://www.vox.com/the-gray-area/459355/china-us-infrastructure-building-housing-high-speed-rail))
- Willingness to fully close roads during construction rather than maintaining traffic throughout (a major time multiplier — one US engineer noted a 6-year bridge project would have taken 2 years if the highway could be closed)

**The cost gap**: US state/local governments spent $266 billion on highways in 2022. On a per-project basis, **US spending is over 3x higher** than comparable upper- and middle-income countries. ([Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/why-does-building-and-maintaining-highways-in-the-us-cost-so-much/))

### Spain: The Efficiency Benchmark

Spain offers a closer comparison — a democracy with labor protections and environmental review — yet builds substantially faster and cheaper than the US.

**Spain highway building record (motorways):**
- 1990: ~4,693 km of motorways
- 2000: ~9,049 km
- 2010: ~14,262 km
- Added ~9,569 km in 20 years — over 478 km/year ([Wikipedia: Evolution of motorway construction in European nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution_of_motorway_construction_in_European_nations))

Spain has the **second-largest high-speed rail network in the world** (after China) and builds it at some of the lowest per-kilometer costs globally. The Spanish Transport Minister attributes this to: economies of scale from continuous network expansion; competitive procurement systems; skilled domestic construction firms who competed for decades of HSR work; and EU co-funding that subsidizes cost.

**European highway construction costs** (for context):
- Germany: €8.24 million/km for flat terrain
- Denmark: €5.89 million/km
- Austria: €12.87 million/km (mountainous)
- US equivalent (flat terrain comparable): roughly $15–30M/km depending on urban/rural context — substantially higher even before factoring in right-of-way costs ([Global Highways study](https://www.globalhighways.com/news/european-highway-construction-costs-evaluated))

### Specific Timeline Comparison

**Dutch highway overpass construction**: Time-lapse videos from 2016 show Dutch crews building an entire highway overpass over a **single weekend**. ([Cato Institute citing this example](https://www.cato.org/commentary/why-does-american-infrastructure-cost-more-take-longer-build-it-used))

**I-35 Capital Express Central (Austin)**: 8 miles of urban highway reconstruction. Planning started late 1980s. Construction started 2024. Estimated completion 2032–2033. Total elapsed time from first study to completion: ~45 years.

**MoPac South (Austin)**: 8-mile express lane addition. Environmental study initiated 2013. Draft Environmental Assessment released 2026. Construction not expected before ~2030 at earliest. **Environmental review alone: 13+ years.**

**China's comparable urban highway reconstructions**: Major urban freeway projects in Beijing, Shanghai, and Shenzhen typically complete in 3–5 years from project approval to ribbon cutting, including tunnels. China's Wuhan Second Yangtze River Bridge and similar projects have been completed in under 3 years.

## 6. Synthesis: Why TxDOT Is Structurally Slow

The slowness is not attributable to a single cause. It is the compound effect of at least seven interlocking mechanisms:

### Mechanism 1: NEPA Creates a Multi-Year Pre-Construction Mandatory Minimum
Any project touching federal funds requires environmental review. EIS for major highway projects has a historical median of 6.7+ years for FHWA. This is non-negotiable and cannot be shortened without federal statutory change. The 7,000-page I-35 DEIS illustrates the documentation burden.

### Mechanism 2: Citizen Litigation Rights Create Strategic Delay
Post-1970 legal reforms (NEPA, Overton Park) allow any citizen to sue to halt construction after any decision point. On MoPac South, a Save Our Springs lawsuit halted the project for over 2 years. Any organized opposition can add 2–5 years to a project timeline through litigation without winning on the merits — the delay itself is the tactic.

### Mechanism 3: Gas Tax Stagnation Creates Funding Uncertainty
Neither the state (last raised 1991) nor federal (last raised 1993) gas tax has been updated in over three decades. In real, inflation-adjusted terms, these revenues buy roughly half what they did when set. The result is that projects wait in queues for funding authorization, then compete in annual UTP cycles. Funding uncertainty delays project advancement — if a project may not receive money, investing in expensive EIS preparation is risky.

### Mechanism 4: Prop 1 and Prop 7 Add Complexity Without Raising the Tax
Texas's workaround (diverting oil/gas severance taxes and sales taxes) provides funding but creates new constraints: Prop 7 is restricted to non-toll roads; Prop 1 is volatile and expires in 2042. Projects requiring toll revenue as part of their funding model (like SH-130) must navigate different authorities and procurement models. The political unwillingness to raise the gas tax means TxDOT must assemble complex multi-source funding packages for every major project.

### Mechanism 5: Davis-Bacon and BABA Add 10–35% to Cost Without Adding Speed
These requirements don't directly slow construction timelines, but they materially increase costs per mile. Higher costs mean fewer projects can be funded from the same pool, extending the effective queue time for any given project. And Davis-Bacon compliance paperwork adds administrative friction throughout construction.

### Mechanism 6: Understaffed TxDOT Outsources to Consultants, Raising Costs and Errors
State DOT employment per capita has fallen dramatically over 20 years nationwide. The research finding is stark: states with higher DOT employment build roads 16% more cheaply. Understaffed agencies outsource to consultants (adding ~20% per lane-mile in costs), provide less detailed bid packages (generating change orders worth $25,000+/lane-mile each), and have less institutional knowledge to drive efficient decisions. TxDOT is not immune to this national trend.

### Mechanism 7: Traffic-Through-Construction Requirement Multiplies Construction Time
Unlike China or some European jurisdictions that close roads entirely during major reconstruction, US projects almost always require maintaining live traffic throughout. This forces multi-phase, multi-year construction staging. One civil engineer's estimate: a 6-year bridge project would have taken 2 years with a full closure. TxDOT's I-35 Central is being built in 6 phases over 8+ years precisely because I-35 cannot be closed.
