## 1. Public School Funding Structure

### The Property Tax Mechanism

Public K–12 education in the United States is funded through a three-source structure: local (primarily property taxes), state aid, and federal grants. In FY 2022–23, the national breakdown was approximately:

- **Federal**: $120.3 billion (~13–15% of total K–12 spending)
- **State**: $422.8 billion (~45–47%)
- **Local**: $403.4 billion (~43%), of which **81% comes from property taxes**

The property tax share has been remarkably stable for decades — never deviating far from ~36% of total funding since the late 1980s, per the [Lincoln Institute of Land Policy](https://www.lincolninst.edu/app/uploads/2024/04/public-schools-property-tax-lla220406.pdf).

The structural problem: property tax revenue is hyperlocal. Wealthier ZIP codes generate more tax revenue at the same or lower tax rate, meaning affluent districts fund excellent schools with relative ease while poor districts struggle at higher tax burdens.

**Key data point**: 81% of all local education funding comes from property taxes (NCES, 2019–20). Districts serving predominantly white students receive $23 billion more than districts predominantly serving students of color, per [EdBuild analysis cited by CBCF](https://www.cbcfinc.org/capstones/education/exploring-the-nexus-of-property-taxes-housing-disparities-and-educational-access-for-black-and-brown-youth-in-major-u-s-cities/).

The gap between the highest-spending state (NY: ~$30K) and the lowest (ID: ~$10–11K) is roughly **3:1**. In raw terms, the wealthiest 25% of districts spend approximately **$1,500 more per student** than the poorest 25%, per [Congressional Black Caucus Foundation research](https://www.cbcfinc.org/capstones/education/exploring-the-nexus-of-property-taxes-housing-disparities-and-educational-access-for-black-and-brown-youth-in-major-u-s-cities/).

**Within-district inequity** compounds this: in New York City, the highest-poverty schools receive **12% less per pupil** than the district's wealthiest schools ([Rockefeller Institute of Government](https://rockinst.org/news/article/new-rockefeller-institute-report-finds-funding-disparities-between-wealthy-and-poor-schools-within-new-yorks-big-5-districts/)). Buffalo's spread is 26%.

### The State Aid Patch — And Its Limits

State aid is theoretically designed to compensate for low local property values, but the mechanism is inconsistent and politically contested. In California, legal reforms following *Serrano v. Priest* (1971) shifted toward equalization — 93% of California students now attend districts with spending differences under $100/pupil for basic instruction — yet equal nominal spending has not produced equal outcomes. In Michigan, per-pupil funding declined 22% in inflation-adjusted terms between 2002–2015, per Michigan State University research cited by [CBCF](https://www.cbcfinc.org/capstones/education/exploring-the-nexus-of-property-taxes-housing-disparities-and-educational-access-for-black-and-brown-youth-in-major-u-s-cities/).

**Federal funding share** has actually *declined* as a percentage over 50 years, falling to only ~8% in 2019–20 before COVID relief temporarily elevated it to ~13–15% ([Learning Policy Institute](https://learningpolicyinstitute.org/blog/how-education-funding-matters-lessons-naep-pandemic-and-recovery-efforts)). Title I — the major federal equity program — is designed to direct dollars toward high-poverty schools, but inadequate funding limits its impact.

## 2. Measurable Outcomes by School Type and Funding Level

### NAEP Scores: The National Benchmark

The National Assessment of Educational Progress (NAEP) is the primary cross-sector comparison tool. Key 2022–2024 findings:

**Overall trend**: Scores declined sharply from 2019 to 2022 (COVID impact), with only partial recovery through 2024. In 2024, 8th-grade reading is at its lowest since 1992; 40% of 4th graders and 33% of 8th graders scored below *NAEP Basic* in reading ([NAGB 2024 NAEP results](https://www.nagb.gov/powered-by-naep/the-2024-nations-report-card/10-takeaways-from-2024-naep-results.html)).

**By school type** (NAEP 2022 8th-grade data):
- **Catholic school 8th graders**: Average reading score **284**; **55%** at or above proficient
- **Public school 8th graders**: Average reading score **264**; **33%** at or above proficient
- **Catholic school math advantage**: ~12 points at 8th grade, ~7 points at 4th grade
- **Source**: [CAPE Network / NCES data](https://capenetwork.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/11/Outlook409.pdf)

8th grade private school students averaged **~20 points higher** than public school students on NAEP reading in 2022 ([US News, citing NAEP data](https://www.usnews.com/education/k12/articles/private-school-vs-public-school)).

**Critical caveat**: Private school advantages largely disappear when controlling for family income and parental education. Private schools draw from more affluent and educated families — selection effects are substantial.

### The Funding-Outcomes Relationship

The relationship between per-pupil spending and NAEP scores is empirically contested:

**Evidence that spending matters**:
- A $1,000/pupil increase in spending for 4+ years improves test scores **91%+ of the time** and raises graduation/college enrollment rates **92%+ of the time** per meta-analysis ([Commonwealth Institute](https://thecommonwealthinstitute.org/tci_blog/money-matters-school-funding-and-student-outcomes/))
- Increasing per-pupil spending by 10% for all 12 school years raises high school graduation probability by **7 percentage points** (10 points for low-income students) ([Learning Policy Institute](https://learningpolicyinstitute.org/blog/how-education-funding-matters-lessons-naep-pandemic-and-recovery-efforts))
- A $1,000 increase in operational spending correlates with **+0.15 standard deviation** in test scores and **+9 percentage point** graduation rate gains, primarily in below-median-spending districts ([Journal of Public Economics, 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0047272720300062))
- Effects are larger for disadvantaged students and in low-spending districts — consistent with diminishing returns

**Evidence that more spending ≠ better outcomes in aggregate**:
- In **36 states**, per-student spending rose since 2003, yet 8th-grade NAEP scores in *both* math and reading declined ([PERC at Texas A&M, 2026](https://perc.tamu.edu/blog/2026/01/school-spending.html))
- Only **6 states** converted higher spending into NAEP gains in both subjects
- High-spending New York, Vermont, Connecticut, and NJ show outcomes close to or below the national average

**Resolution**: The evidence suggests spending *does* raise outcomes in underfunded, high-poverty districts — but additional spending in already well-funded, affluent districts produces minimal gains. The "spending doesn't help" argument is largely driven by aggregate cross-state correlations that mask this inequality.

### ACT/SAT College Readiness Scores

Mean ACT composite scores (approximately 2015–2023 data):

| School Type | Average ACT Composite |
|---|---|
| Private school | 24.2 |
| Homeschool | 22.9 |
| Public school | 20.3 |

Source: [School and Student Services / VenturEd Solutions](https://www.solutionsbysss.com/blog/are-private-schools-ahead-of-public-schools/), citing ACT national data.

Private school students met or exceeded ACT benchmarks in: reading (66% vs. 44%), math (60% vs. 40%), science (55% vs. 36%). Again, demographic selection effects are significant.

Homeschooled students average **SAT 1190** vs. **1060** for public school students — a 130-point gap, per [Crown Counseling research compilation](https://crowncounseling.com/statistics/public-school-vs-homeschool/).

### Graduation Rates by School Type

| School Type | Graduation Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Public school | ~87% | National 4-year rate |
| Private school | ~96% | Selective admissions inflate this figure |
| Charter school | 60–95%+ | Wide variation by network |

Private school graduates enroll in 4-year colleges at **86%** vs. **67%** for public school graduates. Students who attended private school in 8th grade were **twice as likely** to complete a bachelor's degree by their mid-20s (52% vs. 26%) per [Wooclap analysis of NCES data](https://www.wooclap.com/en/blog/public-vs-private-school-statistics/).

**44% of public school students** began 2023–24 behind grade level in at least one subject — rising to **56% in high-poverty neighborhoods** ([NCES data cited by Wooclap](https://www.wooclap.com/en/blog/public-vs-private-school-statistics/)).

## 3. Homeschooling: Regulation, Accountability, and Gaps

### Scale

An estimated **3.7 million students** are homeschooled in the US — approximately 6.6% of the school-age population, growing at ~8% annually ([Crown Counseling enrollment data](https://crowncounseling.com/statistics/public-school-vs-homeschool/)).

### Regulatory Framework: Three Tiers

Homeschool law is entirely state-governed. The [Coalition for Responsible Home Education](https://crhe.org/state-by-state/) and [HSLDA](https://hslda.org) map state requirements into three broad regulatory tiers:

**Low-regulation states** (11 states): Alaska, Idaho, Illinois, Indiana, Michigan, Missouri, New Jersey, Oklahoma, Texas, Iowa, and California (for practical purposes). These states:
- Require **no notification** to any government entity
- Require **no curriculum** submission or approval
- Conduct **no assessments**
- Maintain **no records requirement**

**Moderate-regulation states** (12 states): Maine, New Hampshire, Ohio, Oregon, Washington, North Dakota, Minnesota, North Carolina, South Carolina, Virginia, West Virginia, Hawaii. These typically require:
- Annual notification
- Attendance record maintenance
- Annual assessments (though not always submitted)

**High-regulation states** (5 states): Massachusetts, New York, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Vermont. These require:
- Notice of intent
- Detailed subject lists
- Regular testing or portfolio evaluations
- Quarterly progress reports (NY)
- Curriculum plan review and approval (MA)
- Parent qualifications (diploma/GED in some states)

### The Accountability Gap

Key structural problems identified by [CRHE's 2024 Homeschooling's Invisible Children report](https://crhe.org/new-report-reveals-troubling-insights-on-abuse-of-homeschooled-children/):

- **11 states** require no notification whatsoever — creating effective "truancy loopholes" that nullify compulsory education law
- **18 states** require only notification with no accountability mechanism for whether children are educated
- **47 states** do not prevent caregivers convicted of crimes against children from homeschooling
- **All 50 states** allow families recently subject to child protective services investigations to withdraw children to homeschool
- **Only 1 state** enforces consistent academic progress assessments for all homeschooled children
- **No state** requires all homeschooled children to come into contact with a mandatory reporter for child abuse

CRHE identified **59 cases** where children were withdrawn to homeschool immediately after a social services investigation closed; at least **31 homeschooled children** died from abuse in these circumstances since 2000. **61% of abuse cases** in their database came to light only when victims were dead, dying, or had fled.

### Academic Outcomes: What the Research (and Its Limits) Shows

Studies consistently show homeschooled students score **15–25 percentile points** higher on standardized tests and average **SAT 1190** vs. **1060** for public school peers. NHERI (National Home Education Research Institute) [reviews 62% of peer-reviewed studies](https://nheri.org/homeschool-students-academic-achievement-research/) showing homeschool students outperforming institutional school peers.

**Critical limitations**:
- Studies predominantly rely on **opt-in, non-random samples** — families who voluntarily submit testing data are almost certainly more motivated
- Selection bias is severe: homeschool families have higher median incomes and more educated parents than the general population
- Studies find **white (83%)**, two-parent, higher-income families dominate most datasets
- Causation cannot be established — parental education and family resources (not homeschooling per se) likely drive outcomes
- A **2025 randomized study** suggests homeschooled students may attend college at **far lower rates** than peers despite higher test scores

A 2024 survey of 3,700+ homeschool alumni found **nearly 1 in 4** reported experiencing educational neglect or abuse during homeschooling, per CRHE data cited in NHERI's own literature review.

**Regulatory paradox**: An NHERI study found students in the **least-regulated states** scored higher in some subjects than those in moderate/high-regulation states — used by homeschool advocates to argue against oversight, but interpreted by critics as evidence that the populations choosing homeschool in lax-regulation states differ systematically from those in strict-regulation states.

## 4. Charter School Performance vs. Traditional Public Schools

### The CREDO III Study (2023): Definitive Evidence

The Stanford University [Center for Research on Educational Outcomes (CREDO)](https://ncss3.stanford.edu/executive-summary/full-executive-summary/) released its third national study in June 2023, analyzing data from **1.85 million charter students** at **6,200 schools** across **31 states** from 2014–2019. It is the largest and most methodologically rigorous study of charter performance.

**Top-line findings**:
- Charter students gained **+16 days of learning in reading** and **+6 days in math** per year vs. comparable TPS students
- 83% of charter students performed the same or better in reading; 75% in math
- This reverses findings from CREDO's 2009 study, when charters *underperformed* traditional public schools
- Trend improvement over 15 years: +23 days/year in reading, +37 days/year in math

**Performance by subgroup**:

| Group | Reading gain (days) | Math gain (days) |
|---|---|---|
| Black students | +35 | +29 |
| Black students in poverty | +37 | +36 |
| Hispanic students | +30 | +19 |
| Hispanic students in poverty | +36 | +30 |
| Students in poverty (all) | +23 | +17 |
| English language learners | +6 | +8 |
| Students with disabilities | **–13** | **–14** |
| White/Asian/Native American | ~0 | ~0 to negative |

**Structural differentiators**:
- **Charter Management Organizations (CMOs)**: +27 days reading, +23 days math over TPS — significantly outperforming standalone charters
- **Standalone charter schools**: +10 days reading, ~0 in math
- **Fully online charters**: **–58 days reading, –124 days math** — described as "horrific" by CREDO researchers
- **Urban charter schools**: +29 days reading, +28 days math
- **Rural charter schools**: +5 days reading, **–10 days math**

**By geography** (states with strongest gains):
- Rhode Island: +90 days reading, +88 days math
- New York: +75 days reading, +73 days math
- Tennessee: +33.5 days reading, +39 days math

After 4+ years of continuous enrollment, charter students show **+45 days reading** and **+39 days math** over TPS peers — addressing early concerns about adjustment periods.

### The "Skimming" Question

A persistent critique is that charters attract motivated, higher-achieving students and self-selecting families, inflating apparent performance. CREDO directly addresses this: charter schools in their sample enrolled students who were **disproportionately lower-achieving** than the students in their former TPS — the opposite of creaming/skimming.

### Caveats and Methodological Concerns

The [National Education Policy Center review](https://nepc.colorado.edu/sites/default/files/reviews/NR%20Ferrare_0.pdf) notes:
- CREDO's "matched student" methodology has significant limitations — causal conclusions cannot be drawn from observational data
- "Virtual twins" matching may not fully control for family motivation and engagement
- Charter concentration in urban areas and CMO structures make national averages misleading — highly heterogeneous sector
- Special education performance gap is "consistent and persistent" and not adequately addressed

**36% of charter schools** have stronger growth than local TPS, **47% similar**, **17% weaker** — meaning roughly 1 in 6 charters actively underperforms its comparable public school.

### Graduation Rates

Charter school graduation rates range from **60% to 95%+**, with wide network variation. High-performing CMO networks (KIPP, Success Academy) report rates above 90%. The [DC Public Charter School Board](https://dcpcsb.org/public-charter-school-graduation-rates) data shows 4-year rates ranging from 27% (Goodwill Excel, serving primarily at-risk adults) to 98% (BASIS DC) in SY 2024–25.

## 5. Curriculum Governance: Who Decides What Gets Taught

### The Layered Authority Structure

Curriculum authority in the US is a deeply fragmented, constitutionally complex system with no federal mandate over content:

- **Federal government**: No constitutional authority over curriculum; influence is indirect through funding conditions (Title I, ESEA/ESSA waivers) and voluntary programs (Race to the Top incentivized Common Core adoption). Federal dollars are ~13% of funding.
- **State governments**: Constitutional authority to mandate standards, set curriculum frameworks, and control certification. All 50 states have compulsory attendance laws.
- **Local school districts**: In **45 states**, local entities (districts/schools) develop curricula. In **3 states** (Colorado, North Carolina, Rhode Island), state entities develop curricula directly. In **2 states** (South Carolina, Texas), both levels share authority. ([Ballotpedia, 2023](https://news.ballotpedia.org/2023/09/14/who-sets-public-school-curricula-in-each-state/))

Courts have consistently ruled that school boards have "broad discretion" to implement curriculum; teachers have no constitutional right to override prescribed curriculum.

### Textbook Adoption: The 19-State System

**19 states plus DC** use centralized state-level textbook adoption, where a state board reviews and approves lists of acceptable textbooks for districts. The remaining states leave selection to local districts. ([NASBE](https://www.nasbe.org/what-role-do-states-play-in-selecting-k-12-textbooks/))

Three states exercise **outsized national influence** because of market size:
- **California**: Largest student population; K–8 textbooks adopted by State Board of Education; drives progressive/inclusive content standards
- **Texas**: Second-largest market; traditional state adoption; Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) standards, not Common Core; drives conservative/traditional content
- **Florida**: Growing third influence; 41% of submitted K–12 math textbooks were rejected in 2022 (71% of K–5 materials) for alleged Critical Race Theory, Common Core, and Social Emotional Learning content

Together, California, Florida, and Texas bought ~**25% of all pre-K–12 resource revenue** for publishers in 2020–21. Because publishers cannot afford to produce separate versions for each state, the "small group of people who approve textbooks within a couple of states have a very, very large effect on what students across… the country are able to learn," per [APM Research Lab analysis](https://www.apmresearchlab.org/10x-textbook-adoption).

### Case Study: Texas SBOE (State Board of Education)

The Texas SBOE is a **15-member elected body**, making it directly subject to electoral politics. Its decisions have national effects:

**2023 Science Textbook Controversy**: Texas SBOE members — including board member Will Hickman (a Shell Oil attorney) and Pat Hardy (who co-wrote new rules with the Texas Energy Council) — added requirements that textbooks "present positive aspects of… abundant natural resources" and "avoid bias" on energy. The [National Center for Science Education](https://ncse.ngo/everythings-bigger-texas-including-ideological-attacks-science-textbooks) documented board members explicitly targeting evolution and climate change coverage. Publishers preemptively revised textbooks:
- McGraw-Hill removed human evolutionary lineage diagrams from biology textbooks
- Discovery Education removed fossil fuel–greenhouse effect references from 6th grade materials
- 8 of 22 publishers' textbooks were ultimately rejected

**2026 social studies review**: Ongoing sweeping changes to standards under current SBOE review.

**Texas's non-adoption of Common Core**: Texas (alongside Virginia, Alaska, Nebraska) never adopted Common Core State Standards, instead using its own TEKS. This effectively bifurcates the textbook market.

### Common Core: The Federal Overreach Battle

Between 2010–2014, **46 states and DC** adopted Common Core State Standards, developed through the National Governors Association. The initiative was voluntary but incentivized through Race to the Top federal grants (~$4.35 billion distributed).

Political resistance collapsed adoption:
- Indiana became the first to withdraw (March 2014)
- South Carolina and Oklahoma followed
- 16+ other states passed legislation to repeal or modify
- "Obamacore" branding by Tea Party conservatives framed it as federal overreach despite its voluntary, state-led origin ([Harvard Graduate School of Education analysis](https://www.gse.harvard.edu/ideas/ed-magazine/14/09/what-happened-common-core))

As of 2025, ~150 education-related bills were filed in 32 states targeting curriculum and textbook content — the most active legislative environment in recent decades ([Amnet, 2025](https://amnet.com/how-state-legislation-is-shaping-k-12-textbook-adoption-in-2025/)).

### The Structural Accountability Gap in Curriculum

The decentralized structure produces several systemic problems:
1. **No national coherence**: A student moving between states may encounter entirely different curriculum sequences
2. **Publisher capture**: Textbook publishing is dominated by Pearson, McGraw-Hill, and a handful of others; state adoption battles effectively allow a few hundred state board members to shape content for millions of students
3. **Political volatility**: Elected state boards (like Texas's) are directly subject to ideological campaigns; content decisions are fought as proxy wars over broader culture-war issues
4. **Opacity**: Many states lack meaningful public transparency about textbook review processes; in some, the criteria are established by sub-committees with limited oversight
5. **Adoption lag**: The timing of state adoption cycles — not content quality — is the strongest predictor of how outdated classroom materials are, per [CEMD research](https://www.cemd.org/impact-of-state-adoptions/)

## Summary: Structural Problems Across the System

| Domain | Core Problem | Key Data |
|---|---|---|
| **Funding** | Property tax linkage creates a wealth-based hierarchy | 3:1 spending ratio NY vs. ID; poorest districts get 16% less state/local funding than wealthier ones |
| **Public school outcomes** | Funding helps, but only in underfunded districts; post-pandemic declines are severe | 40% of 4th graders below NAEP Basic in reading (2024); scores still below 2019 |
| **Private school outcomes** | Clear advantage on tests/graduation, but primarily reflects family SES selection | ~20 pt NAEP gap; 96% vs. 87% graduation; 52% vs. 26% bachelor's completion |
| **Homeschool** | Academic outcomes skewed by selection bias; accountability entirely absent in 11 states | 11 states require zero notification; 47 states allow convicted child abusers to homeschool; 61% of abuse cases only discovered after victim death or escape |
| **Charter schools** | Significant gains for Black/Hispanic/poverty students in CMO-run urban schools; online charters are catastrophically harmful | +16 days reading, +6 math overall; online charters –58 days reading, –124 days math |
| **Curriculum** | Textbook market controlled by 3 states; elected boards subject to political capture; no federal coherence; ~150 curriculum bills filed in 32 states in 2025 | TX, FL, CA = ~25% of publisher revenue; 8 of 22 publisher textbooks rejected in TX 2023 on ideological grounds |
