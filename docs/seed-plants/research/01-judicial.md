## 1. Federal Judicial Appointment Structure

### Constitutional Basis
Article III of the U.S. Constitution grants federal judges — including Supreme Court justices — lifetime tenure ("during good Behaviour") subject to presidential nomination and Senate confirmation. This structure was defended by Alexander Hamilton in Federalist No. 78 as essential for judicial independence: "the judiciary's lack of military or financial power made it the least threatening branch." The rationale is that lifetime tenure insulates judges from electoral pressure, allowing them to "apply the law with only justice in mind, and not electoral or political concerns." — [Georgetown Law Journal of Legal Ethics](https://www.law.georgetown.edu/legal-ethics-journal/blog/lifetime-appointments-of-federal-judges-a-double-edged-sword/)

### The Confirmation Process: Growing Politicization Over Time
The Senate's average confirmation timeline has grown dramatically:

| Presidential Term | Average Confirmation Time |
|---|---|
| Reagan 1st Term (1981–84) | ~1 month |
| Clinton 2nd Term (1997–2001) | ~6 months |
| Present (varies by party control) | Often 1–2+ years or never |

Key dynamics:
- Divided party control slows confirmations dramatically. The Senate minority retains power to delay nominees even without majority control.
- "Home state senator" power (blue slips): a home state senator's opposition can functionally block nominees even at the committee stage.
- Strategic vacancy manipulation: Republicans blocked ~100+ Obama judicial nominees in his final two years (2015–16), resulting in only 22 judges confirmed in the 114th Congress — "less than one-fourth the average number confirmed in a two-year period and, as a percentage of the size of the judiciary, the lowest two-year confirmation rate in American history." This created a cache of vacancies for the incoming Trump administration. — [Federalist Society / Heritage Foundation data via Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/the-senate-as-a-black-hole-lessons-learned-from-the-judicial-appointment-experience/)

### Ideological Reshaping Through Appointments

| President | Total Article III Appointments | Appellate Judges | SCOTUS |
|---|---|---|---|
| Obama (8 years) | 329 | 55 | 2 |
| Trump 1st Term (4 years) | 234 | 54 | 3 |
| Biden (4 years) | 228 | 45 | 1 |

When Trump left office, Republican-appointed judges constituted **53.6%** of federal appellate judges, up from 39.7% when he entered — a 14-point swing in four years. — [Brookings Institution / 19th News](https://19thnews.org/2025/01/trump-judges-federal-judiciary/)

Biden's 228 appointments included record levels of diversity: **63%** were women (144 judges), and **37%** (85 judges) were women of color. Trump's appointments were **82% white** and **65% white men**. — [Pew Research Center](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2025/01/09/how-biden-compares-with-other-recent-presidents-in-appointing-federal-judges/)

### Professional Homogeneity of the Federal Bench
- Over **70%** of sitting federal appellate judges come from private practice or federal prosecutor backgrounds.
- Only **~1%** of sitting circuit court judges spent the majority of their career as public defenders or in legal aid.
- Approximately **30%** of circuit court judges were educated at just four law schools: Columbia, Harvard, Stanford, and Yale.
- Zero sitting circuit judges spent the majority of their career with a nonprofit civil rights organization (as of 2020). — [Center for American Progress](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/pipelines-power-encouraging-professional-diversity-federal-appellate-bench/)

## 2. State Judicial Selection Methods

### Overview of Methods
States use five primary methods to select judges, often applied differently at trial vs. appellate levels:

| Method | Description | States Using (Appellate) |
|---|---|---|
| **Partisan elections** | Candidates run with party labels | ~7 states |
| **Nonpartisan elections** | Candidates run without party labels | ~15 states |
| **Merit selection ("Missouri Plan")** | Nominating commission → governor appointment → retention election | 11 classic states (AK, AZ, CO, FL, IN, IA, MO, NE, OK, SD, WY) |
| **Merit-confirmation** | Commission + governor + legislative confirmation | Several states (incl. CA variant) |
| **Gubernatorial/legislative appointment** | Governor or legislature selects directly | ~2 states (legislature only) |

33 states and D.C. use at least some form of appointment. — [Fund for Modern Courts](https://moderncourts.org/programs-advocacy/judicial-selection/methods-of-judicial-selection/)

### Comparative Outcomes by Selection Method
Research on appellate bench characteristics (Judicature, 2021):
- **Merit-confirmation judges** have the most prior judicial experience (75%), most judicial clerkships (32%), most prior prosecution, and most academic credentials. They tend to be older with more years of legal experience.
- **Election-state judges** are significantly **younger**, have less experience, are more likely to have come from private practice, are more likely to have attended in-state law schools.
- **Merit-selection judges** (without confirmation) show characteristics roughly comparable to election states in most dimensions.
- **Gender**: Female judges are significantly more likely in election states than in merit-selection or merit-confirmation states. Merit-selection appears to disadvantage women, possibly due to career path differences.
- Source: [Judicature / Duke Law](https://judicature.duke.edu/articles/picking-judges-how-judicial-selection-methods-affect-diversity-in-state-appellate-courts/)

### Perverse Incentives in Elected Courts: Impact on Decisions
Substantial empirical evidence shows judicial elections distort decision-making:

- **Criminal sentencing**: Trial judges in Pennsylvania and Washington sentence defendants to **longer sentences the closer they are to re-election**.
- **Death penalty**: In states with competitive elections, appointed judges reversed death sentences **26%** of the time; retention-election judges reversed **15%**; competitive-election judges reversed only **11%**.
- **Capital override**: Alabama trial judges override jury verdicts (life → death) **more often in election years**.
- **Television advertising effect**: The more TV campaign ads air in a state, the **less likely justices are to rule in favor of criminal defendants** — across all election types (partisan, nonpartisan, and retention).
- **Campaign contributions effect**: A study of Georgia found campaign contributions influence judicial votes. In Texas, 60% of opinions from 1994–97 were "tainted" because at least one justice took money from a contributor linked to a party or lawyer in the case.
- **Citizens United effect**: In the 23 states that had bans on corporate/union independent expenditures, *Citizens United*'s lifting of those bans is associated with a measurable decrease in justices voting for criminal defendants.
- Source: [Brennan Center for Justice / ACS Skewed Justice Report](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/how-judicial-elections-impact-criminal-cases)

## 3. Politicization: The Federalist Society Pipeline

### Organizational Structure and Scale
The Federalist Society (founded 1982, full name: Federalist Society for Law and Public Policy Studies) operates with:
- Hundreds of law school chapters and lawyer chapters nationally
- Reported **$28 million in assets** (2017)
- Major funders include the Mercer family, Koch brothers network, and the broader Leonard Leo donor apparatus
- Source: [The Week](https://theweek.com/articles/791928/how-conservatives-built-powerful-judicial-pipeline)

### Pipeline Mechanics
The self-reinforcing pipeline works through several interlocking mechanisms:
1. **Law school recruitment**: Society chapters recruit conservative law students early, providing networking with federal judges and elite law firms.
2. **Clerkship preferencing**: Federal judges who are Society members preferentially hire Society-member clerks, who then gain résumé credentials that improve their own chances for judgeships.
3. **Nomination vetting**: The Society functions as an informal but effective vetting body. During Trump's 2016 campaign, he pledged that "all of his judicial appointees would be 'picked by the Federalist Society.'"
4. **Perpetuation**: Justice Clarence Thomas has the highest number of former clerks serving as circuit court judges among all living/recent justices.

### Quantitative Dominance
- **At least 85%** of Trump's appellate judicial nominees in his first term were active Federalist Society members. — [Center for American Progress](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/pipelines-power-encouraging-professional-diversity-federal-appellate-bench/)
- All five Supreme Court justices appointed by Republican presidents (Thomas, Roberts, Alito, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh) have close ties to the Federalist Society.
- Trump's first term produced **234 federal judges** (including 3 SCOTUS justices) — all with lifetime tenure — the majority selected through the Society network. — [F1000Research / PMC structured dataset](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12203041/)

### The Leonard Leo Dark Money Network
Leonard Leo, co-chair of the Federalist Society, operates a sprawling parallel infrastructure of nonprofit groups whose explicit purpose is reshaping the judiciary:

- **$580 million+** had been channeled through Leo's network of "court-capture front groups" before 2022. — [Senator Whitehouse Senate Floor Speech](https://www.whitehouse.senate.gov/news/speeches/scheme-18_leonard-leos-16-billion-payday/)
- In August 2022, **ProPublica and the New York Times** revealed that industrialist Barre Seid donated **$1.6 billion** — the largest known political advocacy donation in U.S. history — to Leo's Marble Freedom Trust, a 501(c)(4) nonprofit not required to disclose donors. — [ProPublica](https://www.propublica.org/article/dark-money-leonard-leo-barre-seid)
- The Judicial Crisis Network (JCN, now called Concord Fund), headed by a former Thomas clerk, has spent **tens of millions** on ads during Supreme Court confirmation fights and is a primary funder of state judicial election influence campaigns.
- JCN/Concord Fund gave **$1.85 million** to the Republican State Leadership Committee (RSLC) in one recent cycle for state judicial races. — [Brennan Center](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/new-money-and-messages-judicial-elections-year)

## 4. Dark Money in State Judicial Elections

### Overall Spending Trajectory
State supreme court election spending has accelerated dramatically:

| Election Cycle | Total Spending | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 2015–16 | ~$80M | Pre-*Citizens United* era trajectory |
| 2019–20 | ~$116M | Previous record |
| 2023–24 | **$157.3M** | 35% above prior record; interest groups outspent candidates for first time |
| 2025 (WI alone) | >$100M | Single race shatters all prior records |

Key structural shift: In 2023–24, for the **first time ever**, direct spending by interest groups ($85M, 54%) exceeded candidate spending ($70M, 45%). — [Brennan Center for Justice, Politics of Judicial Elections 2023-24](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/politics-judicial-elections-2023-24)

### Record-Setting Individual Races

| Race | Year | Total Spending | Key Detail |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wisconsin Supreme Court | 2023 | $51M (then-record) | Flipped court 4-3 liberal; 69 separate groups spent money |
| Pennsylvania Supreme Court | 2023 | $27M (state record) | $4.3M in-kind from group funded by Jeffrey Yass ($16M) |
| North Carolina Supreme Court | 2024 | $10.5M + $2M legal fees | Post-election litigation by losing candidate lasted 6 months |
| Ohio Supreme Court | 2024 | ~$6.7M dark money in ads alone | Nearly $1M RSLC last-minute spend |
| Wisconsin Supreme Court | 2025 | >$100M | Elon Musk contributed >$20M; new all-time record |

### Dark Money Opacity and Key Actors

**Conservative side:**
- **RSLC Judicial Fairness Initiative**: As of early 2025, reported spending **$29M+ total** on state judicial races. Donors revealed only via IRS filings long after elections; $2.9M came from Concord Fund (Leonard Leo entity).
- **Fair Courts America**: Spent $12M in 2023-24; $9M came from a single donor — Republican megadonor **Richard Uihlein** — making it relatively transparent by comparison.
- **Judicial Crisis Network** / Concord Fund: The foundational funder of conservative judicial influence, linked to Koch network.

**Liberal side:**
- **The Justice Project (TJP) affiliates**: $8M to state judicial race groups; major donors include Oklahoma billionaire Lynn Schusterman and groups ultimately linked to **Open Society Foundations** — disclosed only via IRS filings post-election.
- **A Better Wisconsin Together**: $6M; funded by dark money, labor unions, national liberal donors.
- **ACLU / Planned Parenthood**: 25%+ of Montana judicial spending; active in multiple states.

**Key issue**: 30% of all TV ad spots in 2023-24 judicial races mentioned abortion (all pro-protection positions), illustrating that state courts have become proxy battlegrounds for national policy conflicts. — [Brennan Center](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/politics-judicial-elections-2023-24)

### Elon Musk's 2025 Entry into Judicial Elections
The 2025 Wisconsin Supreme Court race (liberal Crawford vs. conservative Schimel) became a watershed:
- Musk's America PAC and Building America's Future contributed **$17M+** to pro-Schimel efforts as of March 25, 2025.
- Musk donated **$3M** directly to the Wisconsin Republican Party.
- The race reached **>$100M total** — more than double the previous national record set just two years prior. — [ProPublica](https://www.propublica.org/article/wisconsin-supreme-court-race-most-expensive-us-history-elon-musk), [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2025/03/29/politics/ad-spending-wisconsin-election-dg)

## 5. Conflicts of Interest and Recusal Failures

### The Structural Enforcement Void
The fundamental problem: **there is no external enforcement mechanism for recusal at the Supreme Court.** Decisions about whether to recuse are made solely by the justice whose impartiality is in question. Until 2023, the Supreme Court was the only federal court without a binding ethics code. Even the 2023 code adopted has no enforcement mechanism — justices self-certify compliance. — [CREW / Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington](https://www.citizensforethics.org/news/analysis/five-supreme-court-justices-recused-from-the-same-case-showing-both-progress-and-work-still-to-be-done/)

### Documented Supreme Court Recusal Failures (2012–2024)
Fix the Court has documented at least 25 specific cases where a justice failed to recuse despite an apparent conflict. A representative sample:

| Case | Justice(s) | Conflict | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Trump v. Anderson* (OT23) | Thomas | Ginni Thomas's involvement in fake electors/election subversion schemes | Did not recuse; case decided 3/4/24 |
| *Moore v. U.S.* (OT23) | Roberts, Thomas, Jackson | Financial stakes in LLCs/partnerships potentially affected by tax ruling | None recused; decided 6/20/24 |
| *Moore v. U.S.* (OT23) | Alito | Attorney on cert petition had interviewed Alito while petition was pending | Did not recuse despite calls; decided 6/20/24 |
| *Americans for Prosperity Foundation v. Bonta* (OT20) | Barrett | AFP spent >$1M on Barrett's confirmation | Did not recuse; argued 4/26/21 |
| *Whitehead v. Netflix* (OT20) | Sotomayor, Gorsuch | Book deals with Penguin Random House (respondent) | Did not recuse; *cert.* denied |
| *Valentine v. PNC Financial Services* (OT20) | Alito | Owns PNC Bank shares | Did not recuse; *cert.* denied 1/11/21 |
| *Kerns v. Caterpillar* (OT19) | Alito | Owns Caterpillar shares | Did not recuse; no action taken |
| *NML Capital v. Argentina* (OT13) | Alito | Accepted luxury Alaska vacation from Paul Singer (NML head) in 2008 | Did not recuse; *cert.* granted 1/10/14 |

Source: [Fix the Court](https://fixthecourt.com/2025/07/recent-times-justice-failed-recuse-despite-clear-conflict-interest/)

**Pattern**: In the vast majority of identified missed recusals, "no further action was taken." There is no procedural mechanism to compel retroactive recusal or impose consequences.

### Clarence Thomas Gift Disclosures: The Most Documented Case
Fix the Court's comprehensive analysis (June 2024):
- Thomas received **103 disclosed gifts** totaling over **$2.4 million** from 2004–2023.
- All other justices combined received only **93 gifts totaling ~$248,000** in the same period.
- An additional **101 "likely gifts"** estimated at **~$1.8 million** — mostly free trips/stays from Harlan Crow and at Bohemian Grove — were not disclosed.
- Including likely gifts: Thomas's total is approximately **$4.2 million** over two decades.
- Gifts from Harlan Crow included: multiple private jet flights, international yacht cruises, at least one real estate transaction ($133,363 paid for Thomas family properties in 2014, not disclosed), and tuition coverage (~$144,400 across six years) for Thomas's grandnephew.
- Seven ethics-law experts told ProPublica that Thomas "appeared to have violated federal law by failing to disclose" the travel. — [Fix the Court](https://fixthecourt.com/2024/06/a-staggering-tally-supreme-court-justices-accepted-hundreds-of-gifts-worth-millions-of-dollars/), [ProPublica](https://www.propublica.org/article/clarence-thomas-gift-disclosures-harlan-crow)

### Samuel Alito: Undisclosed Trip and Recusal Issues
- In a separate ProPublica investigation, Alito accepted an undisclosed luxury fishing trip to Alaska from **Paul Singer**, a Republican megadonor. Singer's hedge fund, Elliott Management, subsequently had business before the Supreme Court. Alito never recused from Singer-related cases.

## 6. Measurable Corruption and Accountability Failures

### Federal Lower Court: The Wall Street Journal Stock Ownership Investigation
A 2021 Wall Street Journal investigation — "the most expansive investigation of judicial stockholdings in the U.S." — found:

- **131 federal judges** (later expanded to 137) failed to disqualify themselves from **685 cases** (later 796) between 2010 and 2018.
- These judges, or their family members, owned stock in a company that was a plaintiff or defendant in the case — a direct violation of a **1974 federal law** prohibiting such participation.
- The judges spanned every region of the country and were appointed by presidents from **Lyndon Johnson to Donald Trump**.
- In approximately **two-thirds** of contested motions in these cases, rulings came down **in favor of the judge's or family's financial interests**.
- Judges and their brokers in some cases **traded shares during the pending litigation**.
- Excuses given included: blaming court clerks, citing misspellings in conflict-screening software, and arguing that money-manager-run accounts don't require recusal.
- As of follow-up reporting, **137 judges in 796 cases** had notified courts of their involvement, making those cases eligible to be reopened.
- Source: [Top Class Actions (summarizing WSJ investigation)](https://topclassactions.com/in-depth/u-s-federal-judges-violated-ethics-laws-hundreds-of-times-over-the-a-decade/)

### Federal Misconduct Complaints: Volume and Dismissal Rates
- In 2021: **1,283 complaints filed** against federal judges. Chief judges dismissed **1,410 complaints in whole or in part** (including some from prior years). The most common dismissal reason: complaint related to "merits of decisions" (1,046 dismissals). — [U.S. Courts, Judicial Business 2021](https://www.uscourts.gov/data-news/reports/statistical-reports/judicial-business-united-states/judicial-business-2021/complaints-against-judges-judicial-business-2021)
- In 2025: **1,850+ ethics complaints** filed against federal judges — a **23% increase** from the prior year. — [National Law Journal / LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/national-law-journal_the-number-of-judicial-misconduct-complaints-activity-7434987400772304896-ekfx)
- The overwhelming majority of complaints are dismissed. Structural critics note that **chief judges dismiss complaints about their judicial colleagues** — the "judges judging judges" problem.

### State Courts: Reuters "Teflon Robe" Investigation
A Reuters investigation of state judicial misconduct (2008–2018) found:
- States disciplined judges in **3,613 cases** over ten years — all handled in **private**, withholding from the public the identities of judges and details of offenses.
- **9 of every 10 sanctioned judges were allowed to return to the bench** after being disciplined.
- Representative cases from Alabama alone:
  - Judge Chaney: Appointed his own son in 200+ cases (earned $105,000 from taxpayer-funded appointments 2015-17); found to have violated ethics rules; stepped down from ethics board but remained judge for 18 months.
  - Judge Marvin Wiggins: Accumulated **4 misconduct charges over 10 years** (nepotism, coercing blood donations from defendants in lieu of unpaid fines, one-sided contact in custody disputes); remained on the bench throughout.
  - Judge Anita Kelly: "Callous indifference" to children in family cases; state appeals judges flagged "nearly three-year delays"; retained due to re-election.
- Source: [Reuters "Teflon Robe" Investigation](https://www.reuters.com/investigates/special-report/usa-judges-misconduct/)

### State Judicial Elections: Pay-to-Play Evidence
- Texas study: **60%** of state supreme court opinions from 1994–97 were "tainted" because at least one of seven justices had received campaign contributions from entities with interests in the case.
- Georgia: Empirical research found campaign contributions directly influence judicial votes (Cann, cited in Federalist Society survey of empirical evidence).
- **46%** of judges themselves believe campaign contributions have "at least a little influence" on their decisions; **56%** believe "judges should be prohibited from presiding over cases when one of the sides has given money to their campaign." — [ACS Skewed Justice](https://www.acslaw.org/analysis/reports/skewed-justice/), [Brennan Center](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/how-judicial-elections-impact-criminal-cases)

## 7. Proposed Structural Reforms and Evidence Base

### Reform 1: 18-Year Term Limits for Supreme Court Justices

**The proposal**: Justices would serve 18 active years on the Supreme Court, then take "senior status" (similar to existing provisions for lower court judges), with vacancies opening on a staggered schedule — roughly one every two years, coinciding with midterm elections.

**Public support**:
- Fox News poll: **78%** of Americans support term limits (up from 66% in 2022).
- AP-NORC poll: **67%** favor term limits or mandatory retirement age, including **82% of Democrats** and **57% of Republicans**.
- Brennan Center survey (2020–2023, 9 nationally representative polls): Term limits score ~**73% overall support** — tied for second most popular of 10 proposed reforms, behind "penalties for noncompliance with Court rulings" (86%).
- **57.5%** of sitting judges polled by the National Judicial College support 18-year term limits. — [Brennan Center](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/public-opinion-term-limits-and-other-supreme-court-reforms), [AP-NORC](https://apnorc.org/ap-norc-poll-2-in-3-in-us-favor-term-limits-for-justices/), [National Judicial College](https://judges.org/news-and-info/many-judges-support-term-limits-for-scotus-but-its-complicated/)

**Bipartisan intellectual support**: The reform was first proposed in 2002 by **Federalist Society co-founder Steven Calabresi** (with liberal constitutional scholar Akhil Reed Amar). John Roberts himself, while a White House attorney, wrote that term limits "would ensure that federal judges would not lose all touch with reality through decades of ivory tower existence."

**Constitutional path**: Legal scholars argue this can be enacted by statute (not requiring a constitutional amendment) by building on existing "senior status" provisions under 28 U.S.C. §371. The Presidential Commission on the Supreme Court (2021) analyzed this pathway, and a bipartisan working group at the American Academy of Arts and Sciences has drafted a proposal it argues would withstand constitutional challenge. — [Center for American Progress](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/combining-ethics-and-term-limits-strengthens-efforts-to-rein-in-a-supreme-court-run-amok/)

**Biden administration action (July 2024)**: President Biden formally endorsed (1) 18-year term limits, (2) a binding ethics code, and (3) a constitutional amendment limiting presidential immunity. — [PBS NewsHour](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/biden-proposes-term-limits-and-ethics-code-for-supreme-court-justices/)

### Reform 2: Binding Ethics Code with Enforcement Mechanism

**Current state**: Until November 2023, the Supreme Court was the **only federal court without a binding code of ethics**. The 2023 code adopted by the Court has no external enforcement mechanism; compliance is self-certified. Lower federal courts have an ethics complaint process, but chief judges dismiss the vast majority of complaints, and "judges judging judges" is structurally compromised. — [CREW](https://www.citizensforethics.org/news/analysis/five-supreme-court-justices-recused-from-the-same-case-showing-both-progress-and-work-still-to-be-done/)

**Proposed legislation (118th Congress)**:
- **Judicial Ethics and Anti-Corruption Act (2023)** (Jayapal/Warren): Requires binding ethics code; bans individual stock ownership by federal judges; heightens recusal transparency; mandates written recusal responses; requires livestreaming. — [Alliance for Justice](https://afj.org/article/the-legislative-paths-to-supreme-court-ethics-and-accountability/)
- **Supreme Court Ethics, Recusal and Transparency (SCERT) Act (2023)** (Whitehouse): Binding ethics code; investigatory mechanism; public disclosure of recusal decisions and explanations.
- **Judicial FOIA Expansion Act (2024)** (Schiff): Extends FOIA to the federal judiciary.

**What actually passed**: The **Courthouse Ethics and Transparency Act** (signed May 2022, Biden) — a more limited reform requiring: (a) financial disclosures posted in a searchable online database within 90 days of filing, and (b) judges follow the same STOCK Act reporting requirements as members of Congress (transactions over $1,000 reported within 45 days). This was bipartisan (Cornyn-Coons). Implementation has been slow: more than a month after the statutory deadline, **~70% of 2024 disclosures still had not been posted online**. — [Sen. Cornyn](https://www.cornyn.senate.gov/news/cornyn-coons-bill-to-enhance-judicial-ethics-and-transparency-requirements-passes-house/), [Fix the Court](https://fixthecourt.com/2025/09/new-disclosures-regs-explain-why-its-taking-so-long-to-post-the-reports/)

### Reform 3: Campaign Finance Disclosure for Judicial Races

**The problem**: *Citizens United* (2010) allowed unlimited independent expenditures in all elections including judicial ones. Dark money groups organized as 501(c) nonprofits are not required to disclose donors. In 2023–24, **$85M** in interest group spending on state judicial races came largely from groups that do not disclose their donors in real time (or in some cases at all). — [Brennan Center](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/politics-judicial-elections-2023-24)

**Evidence base for disclosure**: 
- **76%** of voters believe campaign contributions influence judicial decisions.
- **90%** of voters believe interest groups use campaign contributions to shape judicial policy.
- Empirical research consistently shows that campaign contributions correlate with favorable judicial decisions.
- The current system undermines the "appearance of impartiality" requirement central to judicial legitimacy. — [ACS Skewed Justice](https://www.acslaw.org/analysis/reports/skewed-justice/)

**Reform options**: Robust mandatory real-time disclosure of all independent expenditures in judicial elections; "stand by your ad" requirements for judicial campaigns; direct public financing of judicial campaigns (piloted in some states).

### Reform 4: Expand or Reform the Judicial Conduct System

**Current system's failures**:
- Chief judges dismiss the overwhelming majority of complaints without further review. In 2021, "lacking sufficient evidence" and "frivolous" were cited reasons for 951 and 338 dismissals respectively — though most complaints come from losing litigants or prisoners.
- The Judicial Conduct and Disability Act (1980) governs lower court complaints but explicitly **excludes Supreme Court justices**.
- Reuters found 90% of sanctioned judges return to the bench; secret discipline (3,613 cases over a decade) is the norm.

**Reform options**:
- Create an **independent inspector general for the federal judiciary** — a body outside the judicial branch itself that can investigate, subpoena, and publicly report on misconduct.
- Extend the Judicial Conduct and Disability Act explicitly to Supreme Court justices with an enforcement mechanism (possible via statute).
- Mandate public disclosure of all judicial misconduct proceedings, ending the system of secret discipline.
- Prohibit individual stock ownership by all federal judges (JEACA 2023 proposal; stronger than current rules which only require disclosure).

### Reform 5: Merit Selection Reforms in States

**Evidence**: Research shows judicial elections create measurable perverse incentives — longer sentences near election, lower reversal rates of death sentences, pay-to-play dynamics. However, pure appointment systems have their own accountability problems (less diverse, less representative).

**Emerging evidence on hybrid models**: Merit-confirmation systems (commission + confirmation vote) appear to produce judges with the strongest credentials but may reduce diversity and responsiveness. Retention elections under merit selection systems still show some electoral pressure on decision-making.

**Proposed reforms**:
- Transition partisan judicial elections to nonpartisan merit selection with retention elections.
- Implement robust judicial performance evaluation systems (evidence-based ratings published before retention votes).
- Establish public financing for judicial campaigns in states retaining elections.
- Require judicial recusal standards to be enforced by a body other than the judge whose recusal is in question (structural reform applicable to state and federal courts alike).
