**Research compiled from primary government sources, academic studies, and nonpartisan tracking databases.**

## 1. The Filibuster: When Rhetoric Blocks Governance

### Mechanics

The filibuster is not a constitutional provision—it is an accident of a 1806 Senate rules revision. When Vice President Aaron Burr convinced the Senate that the previous-question motion was "redundant," the chamber inadvertently created the procedural void that allows unlimited debate. The first genuine filibuster did not occur until 1837, when the tactic was already understood as pure obstruction: "the design of the Virginians," wrote Senator William Maclay in 1789, "was to talk away the time, so that we could not get the bill passed."

The modern structural version is more efficient. Since the early 1970s, the "silent filibuster" allows any group of 41 senators to simply *threaten* extended debate — and the majority leader will decline to call a vote. No actual speech is required. Rhetoric is the weapon without even being deployed.

**Key structural mechanics:**
- **Cloture threshold**: Since 1975, ending debate requires 60 of 100 senators — meaning a minority of 41 can block indefinitely.
- **Two-track system** (adopted ~1972): Senate can conduct other business while a filibuster is pending, removing the political cost of obstruction. Senators now filibuster without sacrificing floor time.
- **160+ exceptions** to the 60-vote supermajority have been carved out since 1969 (per Brookings Institution analysis by Molly Reynolds), including budget reconciliation, trade agreements, and military base closures — revealing how extensively the rule constrains "normal" legislation.

**Civil rights as an early target:** Political scientists Sarah Binder and Steven Smith found that of the 30 measures derailed by filibuster between 1917 and 1994, exactly **half involved civil rights** — anti-lynching bills (1922, 1935), the Civil Rights Act of 1957, and legislation barring discrimination in employment, housing, and voting.

### The Structural Weapon Effect

The filibuster transforms rhetoric into a veto mechanism: the *threat* of speech — not speech itself — is now sufficient to kill legislation. The [Roosevelt Institute](https://rooseveltinstitute.org/blog/then-and-now-the-filibuster-obstructs-policies-for-the-people/) documented eight bills since 1947 that would have passed with a simple majority but died on filibuster, including a Right-to-Work repeal (1966) that still has consequences today. The 26 least populous states — representing approximately 21% of the U.S. population — can collectively sustain a filibuster.

## 2. Earmarks and Riders: Rhetoric to Justify Unrelated Spending

### Mechanics

An **earmark** (also called "congressionally directed spending") is a provision in a spending bill that directs a specific dollar amount to a specific project in a specific district, bypassing competitive agency review. A **rider** is any unrelated legislative provision attached to a must-pass bill (appropriations, continuing resolutions, debt ceiling).

The process exploits legislative necessity: must-pass spending bills become vehicles for provisions that could never survive as standalone legislation. The structural logic is:

1. A member proposes a local project
2. The project is embedded in a multi-hundred-billion-dollar omnibus
3. The full bill must pass to fund the government
4. Individual provisions cannot practically be stripped without reopening the whole bill
5. Members who benefit from the earmarks feel obligated to vote for the full package

This creates what the [Congressional Institute](https://www.congressionalinstitute.org/2016/09/15/the-case-for-earmarks-were-they-really-that-bad/) describes as an ad hoc authorization-by-appropriation system: earmarks became the de facto method of prioritizing spending after Congress stopped passing individual authorization bills (which it has not done in sequence since 1994).

### Volume and Scale

- **Citizens Against Government Waste (CAGW)** tracked more than **100,849 earmarks worth over $290 billion** in appropriations legislation from 1991 to 2009.
- FY2008: Congress appropriated **$17.2 billion** in earmarked projects.
- Earmarks were **banned in 2011** following national outrage over the "Bridge to Nowhere."
- Earmarks were **reinstated in 2022** (rebranded as "Community Project Funding") with added transparency requirements.
- FY2023: Congress appropriated **7,396 earmarks totaling $26.1 billion** — a 43.9% increase in count from the prior year. ([FiscalNote](https://fiscalnote.com/blog/earmark-vs-pork-barrel-spending))

### Specific Examples of Rhetorical Justification and Abuse

**The Bridge to Nowhere (Alaska, ~2005):** Formally the "Gravina Island Bridge," this $223–398 million earmark by Sen. Ted Stevens and Rep. Don Young would have connected a town of ~8,000 to an island of ~50 residents. Justified as essential economic infrastructure for a rural community. Became the symbol of pork-barrel waste; cancelled after years of public outcry.

**The Big Dig (Boston, MA):** Began as a $2.8 billion federal highway project; ended at **$14.6 billion** — more than 4× over budget. Justified as urban congestion relief for the entire northeast. Final cost borne by all U.S. taxpayers; benefit accrued almost entirely to Boston.

**Hurricane Sandy Relief Riders (2013):** A $17 billion relief bill for Sandy victims was expanded to include nationwide road repairs, Head Start funding, and Smithsonian roof repairs — unrelated provisions inserted using disaster necessity as rhetorical cover. ([Study.com](https://study.com/academy/lesson/what-is-pork-barrel-spending-definition-examples.html))

**2022 Omnibus examples:**
- Sen. Lisa Murkowski (R-AK): $4 million sewer for the city of Pelican, AK — population 98. Cost per resident: $40,816. Justified as rural infrastructure. ([Heritage Foundation](https://www.heritage.org/budget-and-spending/commentary/boondoggles-7-examples-wasteful-outrageous-earmarks-senate-spending))
- Sens. Collins and King (R/I-ME): $20.5 million for Presque Isle International Airport — the *second-largest* airport earmark in the package, despite serving a tiny regional market.

**The logrolling mechanism:** Members justify supporting each other's earmarks with explicit vote-trading. As Senators Ted Stevens and Don Young argued in the Bridge to Nowhere debate: "we've always supported everyone else's earmarks" — a rhetorical framework that transforms local pork into a norm of congressional collegiality.

## 3. Campaign Rhetoric vs. Voting Record: The Measurable Gap

### PolitiFact Promise Trackers

PolitiFact has systematically tracked presidential campaign promises since 2009, developing individual meters for each president. The methodology rates promises on verifiable outcomes — not intentions or effort — across six categories: Promise Kept, Compromise, Promise Broken, Stalled, In the Works, or Not Yet Rated.

**Presidential report cards (final ratings at end of term):**

| President | Promises Tracked | Kept | Compromise | Broken | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Obama (2009–2017) | 533 | ~47% | ~27% | ~23% | Obamameter |
| Trump 1st term (2017–2021) | 102 | ~24% | ~22% | ~53% | Trump-O-Meter |
| Biden (2021–2025) | 99 | ~33% | ~28% | ~35% | Biden Promise Tracker |
| Trump 2nd term (2025–) | Ongoing | ~20% | — | — | MAGA-Meter (partial) |

*Sources: [PolitiFact Obameter](https://www.politifact.com/truth-o-meter/promises/obameter/), [Trump-O-Meter](https://www.politifact.com/truth-o-meter/promises/trumpometer/), [Biden Promise Tracker](https://www.politifact.com/truth-o-meter/promises/biden-promise-tracker/), [MAGA-Meter](https://www.politifact.com/truth-o-meter/promises/maga-meter-tracking-donald-trumps-2024-promises/)*

**Key finding from PolitiFact's methodology director:** "Trump had 53% of promises broken, Biden had 34%, and Obama had 23%." ([National Press Club Journalism Institute](https://www.pressclubinstitute.org/2025/02/06/how-politifact-uses-the-maga-meter-to-monitor-presidential-campaign-promises/))

A separate analysis cited by [The Fulcrum](https://thefulcrum.us/presidential-promises-promises-promises) found that Trump's 663 campaign promises during the 2016 race resulted in fulfillment of approximately 23% by end of term. His 2024 campaign recycled many of the same unfulfilled pledges — a structural repetition that highlights how unaccountable campaign rhetoric is.

### The Structural Explanation for Broken Promises

PolitiFact's analysis reveals that the most common failure mode is not deception but structural limitation: "Trump's unfulfilled campaign promises are broken most often because he would have needed to change laws to achieve his goals but was unable to get action through Congress." This means that campaign rhetoric systematically promises outcomes that require legislative supermajorities the candidate cannot guarantee — making much of campaign speech categorically non-deliverable even under perfect faith.

### Academic Research: Campaign Promises Do Work Electorally

A 2025 study published in *Comparative Political Studies* ([CEPR](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/campaign-promises-political-coupons-future-benefits-sway-dont-retain-voters)) exploiting a natural experiment in Germany found that a well-timed campaign promise boosted party vote share by 12+ percentage points among targeted voters — but the effect **vanished entirely** after the promise was fulfilled. Campaign promises function as "political coupons: valid once, quickly discarded after use." This incentivizes escalating promises rather than durable policy.

### Voting Record Tracking Tools

- **GovTrack.us** and **Congress.gov**: Track all congressional votes, bill sponsorships, and co-sponsorships — allowing direct comparison of legislative record to campaign positions.
- **VoteSmart.org**: Aggregates voting records across offices.
- **The Fulcrum** analysis: "After failing to deliver on his major pledges during the first administration, president-elect Trump resurrected many of the same promises in his 2024 campaign" — illustrating that recycling undelivered rhetoric faces no electoral penalty.

## 4. Lobbying: How Rhetoric Translates to Legislative Text

### Mechanics of Influence

Lobbying works through several channels, each with a rhetorical layer:

1. **Direct drafting**: Lobbyists write actual legislative language. The most documented example: in 2013, 70 of 85 lines in a House Dodd-Frank rollback bill were traced directly to draft language submitted by Citigroup lobbyists. Two paragraphs were copied nearly verbatim, with only minor grammatical changes. ([NPR](https://www.npr.org/sections/itsallpolitics/2013/11/11/243973620/when-lobbyists-literally-write-the-bill))

2. **Model legislation pipelines**: The American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC) convenes corporate representatives and state legislators to write "model bills" — template legislation that members can introduce in any state. ALEC's approximately 200 model bills become law annually. From 2010 to 2018, ALEC bills were introduced nearly **2,900 times** across all 50 states and Congress, with more than **600 becoming law**. ([Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/alecs-influence-over-lawmaking-in-state-legislatures/), [ALEC Exposed/Common Cause](https://www.commoncause.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/08/alecinflorida.pdf))

3. **Talking points bundling**: Lobbyists package legislative language with ready-made floor speeches, co-sponsor recruitment, and press release templates — reducing the transaction cost for members to adopt industry positions wholesale.

4. **ROI on political spending**: One study found that for every dollar spent on lobbying, the most politically active corporations received an average of **$760 in government benefits** — a 76,000% return on investment. ([RepresentUs](https://represent.us/action/5-facts-lobbyists/))

**Top sectors (2023):** Health ($739M+), Finance/Insurance/Real Estate, Miscellaneous Business, and Communications/Electronics.

### The Revolving Door: Structural Capture

The "revolving door" describes the movement of lawmakers and their staff between Congress and the lobbying industry — creating a structurally embedded exchange where the rhetorical value of congressional access is monetized.

**Data:**
- In the 1970s, fewer than **5%** of retiring legislators became lobbyists.
- Today, approximately **half of retiring senators** and **a third of retiring House members** go on to lobbying roles. ([RepresentUs](https://represent.us/action/5-facts-lobbyists/))
- Of former members who left office in January 2019, **more than 50%** became lobbyists or similarly-employed advisors. ([Rep. Jared Golden press release](http://golden.house.gov/media/press-releases/golden-cicilline-introduce-lifetime-lobbying-ban-former-members-congress))
- From the 115th Congress (members departing in January 2019), OpenSecrets tracked **~49% moving to lobbying firms** and another ~11% moving to lobbying clients.
- In 2025, **866 members of Congress and congressional staffers** transitioned from Capitol Hill to K Street — a **60% increase** over 2024 and a new record, exceeding the previous high of 777 set in 2007. ([LegiStorm](https://info.legistorm.com/blog/revolving-door-in-congress))

**The salary premium:** Former members of Congress who become lobbyists receive, on average, a **1,452% raise** over their congressional salary. ([RepresentUs](https://represent.us/action/5-facts-lobbyists/))

**The "cooling off" period** (currently 1–2 years) is widely circumvented through "strategic advising" roles — lobbyists in all but registered name.

## 5. Executive Orders: Rhetorical Durability vs. Policy Durability

### Mechanics and Structural Purpose

Executive orders are directives to the executive branch carrying the force of law, issued under constitutional or congressionally-delegated authority. They are structurally useful as rhetorical instruments for several reasons:

- They require no congressional approval and can be issued unilaterally
- They produce immediate, visible policy "wins" amenable to announcement rhetoric
- They are reversible by the same or any successor president — making them structurally impermanent
- Their *outcome durability* often exceeds their formal durability: physical infrastructure built, contracts awarded, and legal positions taken can be practically irreversible even after formal revocation

### Volume by Administration

*Source: [Pew Research Center](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2025/12/16/trump-has-already-issued-more-executive-orders-in-his-second-term-than-in-his-first/), [American Presidency Project](https://www.presidency.ucsb.edu/)*

| President | Term | EOs Issued |
|---|---|---|
| FDR | 1937–1941 | 1,112 |
| Truman | 1945–1949 | 504 |
| Carter | 1977–1981 | 320 |
| Clinton | 1993–1997 | 200 |
| Reagan | 1981–1985 | 213 |
| GW Bush | 2001–2005 | 173 |
| Obama | 2009–2013 | 147 |
| Biden | 2021–2025 | 162 |
| Trump 1st | 2017–2021 | 220 |
| Trump 2nd | 2025–(partial) | 221+ |

**Median EOs per term (FDR 2nd term through Biden):** ~200. Trump surpassed this median in just the first 8 months of his second term, issuing 26 on his first day back in office alone.

### Reversal Rates: A Quantitative Look

**The Biden-to-Trump reversal (2025):**
- Biden issued **162 executive orders** during his term.
- Trump rescinded **78 on Day One** (January 20, 2025), then 19 more in March 2025.
- Total Biden EOs revoked: **67 of 162 (41%)** as of late January 2025. ([Wikipedia, List of executive actions by Joe Biden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_executive_actions_by_Joe_Biden))
- Trump also revoked 11 Biden presidential memoranda.

**The Obama-to-Trump reversal (2017):**
- Trump revoked 11 Obama orders related to climate change, labor protections, and law enforcement within his first year. ([Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/regulatory-delay-across-administrations/))

**Historical pattern:** A 2024 study published in the *Journal of Public Policy* ([Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-public-policy/article/executive-action-that-lasts/AABF3ACB297FA8B252507582A9B4B68E)) analyzing all ~6,500 executive orders from 1937 to 2021 found:
- **~75% of all executive orders have never been formally revoked** — but this reflects orders that were simply superseded, expired, or became moot, not that they endured under hostile administrations.
- Orders are **~9 percentage points less likely to be revoked** when they involve "immutable outcomes" — physical assets built, personnel reassigned, land designated.
- Party turnover in the presidency reliably triggers formal reversal of mutable-consequence orders.

**The structural paradox:** Executive orders are simultaneously the *most reversible* formal policy instrument and the *most rhetorically powerful* because they are immediate. Administrations use them to project decisive action on issues where legislation is blocked — knowing the next president can undo them. This creates a ratchet of theatrical governance: each administration spends significant time issuing and un-issuing orders rather than producing durable law.

### Why EOs Persist as Rhetorical Tools

The filibuster-driven legislative gridlock (see Section 1) makes executive orders structurally necessary. If 60 Senate votes are required to pass any significant legislation, presidents are forced into unilateral action. EOs become the only visible policy output available to a president who lacks a supermajority — and the public announcement of each EO functions as its own rhetorical event, distinct from the policy's durability.

## 6. Congressional Hearings: Theater and Its Outcomes

### The Hearing as Performance

Congressional hearings serve multiple distinct functions — only one of which is legislative. The others are political: generating media coverage, signaling to constituents, providing members a platform for rhetoric, and building opposition research. The [Brookings Institution](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/how-partisan-and-policy-dynamics-shape-congressional-oversight-in-the-post-trump-era/) found that as committees have lost control of the legislative agenda to party leaders, they have increasingly redirected energy toward hearings that provide political dividends without requiring floor time.

### The Hearings-to-Legislation Conversion Rate

**Overall legislative productivity:** Over the last decade, Congress has enacted approximately **5.5% of all bills introduced**. ([Quorum](https://www.quorum.us/data-driven-insights/state-legislatures-versus-congress-which-is-more-productive/)) For context, state legislatures enact roughly 25.8% — nearly five times the rate. From 1987 to 2024, only about **2.4% of introduced bills became law**, per [Everything Policy](https://www.everythingpolicy.org/policy-briefs/congressional-lawmaking).

**Hearings vs. legislation specifics:**
- In the 117th Congress (2021–22), approximately **19% of House committee hearings** involved oversight of the executive branch. ([Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/how-partisan-and-policy-dynamics-shape-congressional-oversight-in-the-post-trump-era/))
- Of all non-legislative hearings in a 50-year sample (1969–2018), **nearly 45% involved no genuine oversight** — no scrutiny of specific agency decisions. ([Levin Center PDF](https://levin-center.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/Testimony-Manuscript-Revision-MacDonald-2023.pdf))
- A GMU study found the **median legislator participates in only 25%** of committee hearings their committees hold — and only **~17% of oversight-specific hearings**. ([GMU paper on Extremists and Congressional Oversight](https://administrativestate.gmu.edu/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/Napolio-Rezaee-Extremists-and-Participation-in-Congressional-Oversight-Hearings.pdf))

**What hearings actually produce:** A USC/APSR study ([Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/american-political-science-review/article/efficacy-of-congressional-oversight/97E953579A6DFA76120CAE956522A862)) tracking the effect of congressional hearings on one measurable outcome (improper payments by federal agencies) found:
- Hearings *do* produce a small subsequent decline in the targeted behavior: approximately **0.1 percentage points per year** per agency hearing.
- But this effect is "an order of magnitude smaller" than the threshold OMB considers a "successful reduction" (1% annual decrease).
- Improper payment rates were not meaningfully lower in 2021 than in 2003, despite two decades of hearings.
- **The effect doubles under unified government** — suggesting that hearings are most effective when both the legislature and executive are aligned, i.e., when they are least needed as adversarial oversight.

**Testimony's self-reported influence:** A survey of state legislators found that only **6% said testimony changes their vote**. The majority (81%) said testimony was at least somewhat influential — but defined "influence" primarily as raising awareness or motivating additional research, not changing legislative outcomes. ([PMC/International Journal of Health Policy and Management](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4322632/))

### The Escalating Oversight Theater Dynamic

Brookings documents that total non-legislative hearing activity has remained "relatively constant since the 1980s" — but represents an **increasing share of committee activity** as legislative hearings have declined. In the 117th Congress, House committees held about **20% fewer executive oversight hearings** than in the 116th — despite the January 6th Committee generating enormous public attention. The Senate dedicated just **13% of hearings** to executive oversight.

**Congressional investigations:** Most congressional investigations — even high-profile ones — do not produce legislation. They produce:
- Reports
- Criminal referrals (rarely acted on)
- Appropriations riders (see Section 2)
- Media cycles that benefit members' re-election

The committee "graveyard" is structural: most bills are referred to committee and **never scheduled for a hearing** — let alone a vote. The 118th Congress introduced 19,316 bills and enacted 274 (a **1.4% rate**). The 119th's first year saw ~10,500 bills introduced and ~145 laws enacted (~1.3%).

## Summary: The Rhetoric Architecture

The six mechanisms documented here form an interlocking system in which rhetoric substitutes for, delays, or undermines governance:

| Mechanism | Structural Role | Key Metric |
|---|---|---|
| Filibuster | Transforms speech-threat into a veto; forces supermajority for any legislation | 3,131 total cloture motions; 336 in a single Congress (117th) |
| Earmarks/Riders | Attaches unaccountable local spending to must-pass bills via narrative justification | $26.1B in 7,396 earmarks in FY2023 alone |
| Campaign promises | Commits candidates to outcomes requiring legislative action they cannot guarantee | 23–53% promise-broken rate across recent presidents |
| Lobbying/revolving door | Industry rhetoric becomes legislative text; access monetized at 1,452% pay premium | $4.5B spent in 2024; 866 Hill-to-K-Street transitions in 2025 alone |
| Executive orders | Produce rhetorical "wins" with limited durability; ~41% reversed on administration change | 75% never formally revoked, but partisan reversals systematic |
| Congressional hearings | Generate political visibility with limited enforcement or legislative consequence | <2.5% of bills enacted; 45% of oversight hearings involve no genuine agency scrutiny |
