# Religious Influence on US Governance: Structural Mechanisms and Measurable Impacts

## Overview

This document catalogs documented, source-cited evidence of how religious organizations structurally influence US legislation and policy, where religious doctrine measurably conflicts with evidence-based policy, and where formal church-state separation has broken down.

---

## 1. Religious Lobbying: Structural Mechanisms and Spending

### 1.1 The Tax Exemption Framework

Religious organizations in the United States operate under a uniquely advantaged legal structure:

- **Automatic 501(c)(3) status**: Unlike every other nonprofit category, churches receive federal tax-exempt status automatically — they do not file Form 1023 for recognition. ([IRS / MinistryWatch](https://ministrywatch.com/irs-data-book-reveals-size-scope-of-nations-tax-exempt-religious-nonprofits/))
- **No Form 990 filing**: Churches are exempt from the public disclosure tax returns that all other nonprofits must file. This makes tracking their finances structurally opaque. ([Center for Public Integrity](https://publicintegrity.org/health/catholic-bishops-abortion-war-chest-stays-secret/))
- **Exempt from the Lobbying Disclosure Act**: Churches and religious affiliates are also exempt from the 1995 Lobby Disclosure Act, which requires lobbyists to file disclosure forms detailing their spending and naming their lobbyists. This is distinct from and additional to the IRS exemption. ([Center for Public Integrity](https://publicintegrity.org/health/catholic-bishops-abortion-war-chest-stays-secret/))
- **Property tax exemption**: All 50 states and Washington, D.C. exempt houses of worship from property taxes. Estimates of total tax-exempt religious property value range from $300 billion to $600 billion. NYC alone forgoes approximately $627 million per year in property tax revenue from religious institutions. The total annual value of all religious tax exemptions has been estimated at $71–83.5 billion. ([PoliMetrics/Substack, citing Cragun et al.](https://polimetrics.substack.com/p/how-much-revenue-would-taxing-churches))

### 1.2 The Johnson Amendment: A Structural Loophole

The **Johnson Amendment** (1954, IRC §501(c)(3)) prohibits tax-exempt organizations from endorsing or opposing political candidates. In exchange for this prohibition, organizations receive federal tax exemption and the ability to receive tax-deductible contributions.

**Current status (2025):** The Trump administration's IRS entered into a consent agreement in July 2025 with plaintiffs in *National Religious Broadcasters v. IRS* — agreeing that endorsements by religious leaders delivered through "customary channels of communication" to congregations would not be treated as campaign intervention. This is the first time the IRS formally yielded on the Johnson Amendment's scope, effectively allowing houses of worship to retain tax-exempt status while endorsing candidates. Congress, which is the only branch with statutory authority to modify the law, was bypassed. A federal court later dismissed the case, but the IRS enforcement posture had already shifted. ([Campaign Legal Center](https://campaignlegal.org/cases-actions/fighting-dark-money-defending-johnson-amendment-national-religious-broadcasters-et-al); [Interfaith Alliance](https://interfaithalliance.org/post/what-is-happening-with-the-johnson-amendment))

**Key risk:** If religious 501(c)(3)s are treated as exempt from Johnson Amendment enforcement, they become a new class of dark-money vehicles — able to accept unlimited, undisclosed, tax-deductible donations for political campaigns. Unlike 501(c)(4) social welfare organizations (which cannot offer charitable deductions), religious 501(c)(3)s would offer donors a tax benefit for secret political spending. ([Campaign Legal Center](https://campaignlegal.org/cases-actions/fighting-dark-money-defending-johnson-amendment-national-religious-broadcasters-et-al))

### 1.4 Faith and Freedom Coalition (FFC): Voter Mobilization at Scale

FFC (founded 2009 by Ralph Reed, registered 501(c)(4)) operates primarily outside formal lobbying:

- In **2024**, FFC led a **$62 million plan** to register and turn out faith-based voters, targeting 125,000 churches across battleground states with 30 million pieces of literature. ([FFC website](https://www.ffcoalition.com/voter-education/))
- In **2022**, FFC conducted **180.7 million voter contacts** across the midterm election cycle. ([FFC website](https://www.ffcoalition.com/faith-freedom-coalition-knocked-on-8-2-million-doors-prior-to-midterm-elections/))
- As a 501(c)(4), FFC is not required to disclose donors and can receive unlimited donations. It can also move anonymous money to SuperPACs. ([SourceWatch](https://www.sourcewatch.org/index.php/Faith_and_Freedom_Coalition))
- FFC's formal OpenSecrets contributions profile for 2024 shows only $958 in reported contributions — illustrating how 501(c)(4) voter mobilization at tens of millions of dollars operates largely outside FEC disclosure. ([OpenSecrets](https://www.opensecrets.org/orgs/faith-freedom-coalition/summary?id=D000061215))

### 1.5 Catholic Church Ballot Measure Spending

While prohibited from endorsing candidates, churches may spend on issue advocacy:

- In 2024, Catholic organizations spent **more than $1.9 million** opposing abortion-rights ballot measures in five states, including **$1.1 million in Florida** alone opposing Amendment 4. ([Mother Jones / National Catholic Reporter](https://www.motherjones.com/politics/2024/10/catholic-church-abortion-ballot-measures-florida-colorado-south-dakota-missouri/))
- Between 2004 and 2021, the USCCB and its affiliates spent **no less than $10 million** lobbying state legislators on abortion-related legislation. ([Rolling Stone, citing Catholics for Choice report](https://www.rollingstone.com/politics/politics-news/catholic-church-roe-wade-abortion-kansas-michigan-1234589927/))

## 2. Religious Doctrine vs. Evidence-Based Policy

### 2.1 Abstinence-Only Sex Education

**Federal funding history:**
- Since 1981, the federal government has funded abstinence-only-until-marriage (AOUM) programs through five separate funding streams.
- Between **1996 and FY2018**, Congress funneled **over $2.2 billion** in taxpayer dollars into AOUM programs. ([SIECUS](https://siecus.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/AOUM-Funding-History-Report-5.2019.pdf))
- In **FY2024**, 38% of the $286 million total federal teen pregnancy prevention budget — approximately **$110 million** — went exclusively to Sexual Risk Avoidance (abstinence-only) programs. ([KFF](https://www.kff.org/womens-health-policy/sex-education-programs-definitions-funding-and-impact-on-teen-sexual-health/))

**What the evidence shows:**

| Program Type | Evidence of Effectiveness |
|---|---|
| Abstinence-only (SRAE/AOUM) | A 2017 Cochrane meta-analysis of 13 programs found **no impact** on sexual initiation, frequency of sex, number of partners, condom use, or unprotected sex. CDC found "insufficient scientific evidence" for behavioral change. |
| Comprehensive sex education | CDC meta-analysis of 66 programs found positive effects on frequency of sex, number of partners, unprotected sex, contraceptive use, teen pregnancy, and STI rates. |
| State-level correlation | States requiring abstinence-only education are associated with **higher STI rates**. Of the 10 states with the highest gonorrhea rates among teenagers, 9 require abstinence-only education. |

Sources: [KFF](https://www.kff.org/womens-health-policy/sex-education-programs-definitions-funding-and-impact-on-teen-sexual-health/); [University of Arkansas systematic review](https://scholarworks.uark.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1165&context=nursuht); [ACLU](https://www.aclu.org/documents/what-research-shows-government-funded-abstinence-only-programs-dont-make-grade)

A rigorous multi-year congressional evaluation of four federally funded programs studied more than 2,000 students and found that abstinence-only participants were **just as likely to have sex before marriage** as non-participants, with first intercourse at the same mean age and the same number of sexual partners. ([Mathematica Policy Research, cited in ACLU](https://www.aclu.org/documents/what-research-shows-government-funded-abstinence-only-programs-dont-make-grade))

**Religious motivation:** AOUM programs are rooted primarily in religiously affiliated advocacy. SRAE programs often prohibit teaching the benefits of condoms and contraception, a restriction with no scientific basis. ([SIECUS](https://siecus.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/AOUM-Funding-History-Report-5.2019.pdf))

### 2.2 Federal Embryonic Stem Cell Research Restrictions

- President George W. Bush's **August 9, 2001 ban** on federal funding of new embryonic stem cell lines was widely documented as religiously motivated — based on the belief that a fertilized embryo constitutes a human life from the moment of fertilization.
- Legal scholars have argued the ban violated the Establishment Clause by basing a public policy on the religious beliefs of one minority — evangelical Protestants and pro-life Catholics — over secular scientific judgment. ([Semantic Scholar / law review](https://pdfs.semanticscholar.org/9b9e/f030079ea9dbc2662ff0fa9612dc4d470288.pdf))
- Bush's ban limited research to the approximately 60 existing cell lines at the time, most of which were degraded or unusable. Scientists warned it materially delayed research into treatments for Parkinson's disease, diabetes, spinal cord injuries, and other conditions.
- The ban was partially lifted by President Obama in 2009 via executive order, though the Dickey-Wicker Amendment (1995, Congress) still prohibits federal funds for creating embryos for research or destroying embryos — a continuing statutory restriction. ([GaBIJ](https://gabi-journal.net/the-impact-of-religion-on-human-embryonic-stem-cell-regulations-comparison-between-the-uk-germany-and-the-us.html))
- The legal standing of federally funded embryonic stem cell research has remained uncertain, constituting a multi-decade policy disruption traceable to religiously motivated executive and legislative action. ([GaBIJ](https://gabi-journal.net/the-impact-of-religion-on-human-embryonic-stem-cell-regulations-comparison-between-the-uk-germany-and-the-us.html))

### 2.3 Blue Laws Still in Force (2024)

Blue laws — Sunday commerce restrictions rooted in Christian Sabbatarian practice — remain active across dozens of states:

**Alcohol:**
- **Texas**: Liquor stores closed all day Sunday; beer/wine sales restricted until 10 a.m.
- **Mississippi, North Carolina, Utah**: Liquor stores closed Sundays
- **Alabama, Arkansas, Georgia, Idaho, Kentucky, South Carolina**: Sunday alcohol sales prohibited in some counties (local option)
- **Indiana**: No cold beer in grocery stores (recently changed); Sunday sales only recently liberalized

**Vehicle sales:**
- Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, North Dakota, Oklahoma, and Pennsylvania prohibit car dealership sales on Sundays under state law.
- In Texas, dealerships must close either Saturday or Sunday. ([World Population Review](https://worldpopulationreview.com/state-rankings/blue-laws-by-state))

**Hunting:**
- Connecticut, Maine, North Carolina, Pennsylvania, and West Virginia prohibit or significantly restrict Sunday hunting. ([World Population Review](https://worldpopulationreview.com/state-rankings/blue-laws-by-state))

**Retail:**
- Bergen County, New Jersey (Paramus) prohibits the sale of most goods on Sundays, one of the strictest surviving blue law jurisdictions in the country. ([World Population Review](https://worldpopulationreview.com/state-rankings/blue-laws-by-state))

These laws impose economic costs on non-religious citizens and businesses with no secular rationale. They are enforced through government power but serve exclusively religious purposes.

## 3. "In God We Trust," Religious Oaths, and Non-Religious Citizens

### 3.1 "In God We Trust" as National Motto

- Adopted in 1956 (signed by President Eisenhower) as the official national motto, replacing the de facto motto "E Pluribus Unum."
- Adopted explicitly in Cold War context as an ideological counter to "godless communism."
- Courts have consistently upheld its constitutionality under a "ceremonial deism" doctrine, finding it has become so pervasive as to be stripped of religious significance. The Supreme Court declined review in 2011 after the 9th Circuit upheld dismissal of a challenge. ([Courthouse News](https://www.courthousenews.com/supreme-court-declines-in-god-we-trust-protest/))
- SCOTUS rejected a challenge in 2018, holding that the phrase "does not compel citizens to engage in religion." ([University of Indianapolis Reflector](https://reflector.uindy.edu/2023/08/23/in-god-we-trust-is-unconstitutional-and-problematic/))
- A 2011-2012 House Judiciary Committee resolution, sponsored by Rep. Randy Forbes (R-VA) and 64 co-sponsors, reaffirmed the motto and encouraged its public display in all government buildings and public schools.

**Policy impact on non-religious citizens:** No direct policy outcome can be falsifiably attributed to the motto itself. Its measurable effects are symbolic and structural: atheists and non-monotheistic citizens are excluded from the national self-description. The motto's framing normalizes monotheism as a precondition of civic identity.

### 3.2 Religious Oaths for Government Service

**Federal oath:** All approximately 1.8 million permanent federal employees are required to swear an oath. The OPM has historically modified the oath for some religious groups (e.g., Quakers). A 1999 ACLU lawsuit involved a woman who lost her federal job for refusing to sign an oath on religious grounds. A University of Virginia law professor assessed the suit had good prospects because the accommodation of some religions but not atheists "favors one religion over another." ([ACLU](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/former-us-employee-sues-over-loyalty-oath))

**State-level:** South Carolina law mandates all poll workers swear an oath ending with "so help me God." In 2023, veteran James Reel — an atheist who completed all required training — was barred from serving as a poll worker because he refused the God clause. The Freedom From Religion Foundation filed suit, citing *Torcaso v. Watkins* (1961), in which the Supreme Court held that religious tests cannot be required for public office. The South Carolina Election Commission's response: the County Boards "must require" the full oath. ([Universal Life Church Monastery](https://www.themonastery.org/blog/no-god-no-job-atheist-sues-state-over-religious-oath-requirement))

**Religious oaths cutting the other way:** In *Bolden-Hardge v. Office of the California State Controller*, a Jehovah's Witness lost a job promotion after refusing to swear primary allegiance to state and federal constitutions (over her allegiance to God). The Ninth Circuit found this created presumptive violations of Title VII and Free Exercise Clauses — illustrating that government oaths create competing religious-freedom conflicts in both directions. ([AW Attorneys](https://www.awattorneys.com/client-updates/religious-objection-could-trump-oath-to-uphold-state-and-federal-constitutions/))

### 3.3 Congressional Chaplains

- Both the House and Senate maintain paid government chaplains, compensated at Level IV of the Executive Schedule (approximately $172,500–$174,000 per year as of recent years).
- Combined annual cost to taxpayers: approximately **$800,000+** including staff, offices, and expenses. ([Andrew Seidel / andrewlseidel.com](https://andrewlseidel.com/wasteful-spending-congress-pays-clergy-66000hour-to-pray/))
- Since 2000, Congress has spent **more than $10 million** on chaplain prayers, over 96% of which in the House were directed to the Christian God. ([Andrew Seidel](https://andrewlseidel.com/wasteful-spending-congress-pays-clergy-66000hour-to-pray/))
- As of 2024, all Senate chaplains in US history have belonged to Christian denominations, though no formal rule requires this. ([Wikipedia / Chaplain of the United States Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaplain_of_the_United_States_Senate))
- Legislative prayer at government meetings was upheld in *Town of Greece v. Galloway* (2014, 5-4) as constitutional even when the prayers are predominantly Christian, provided no coercion and no discrimination in selecting prayer-givers. ([SCOTUSblog](https://www.scotusblog.com/cases/case-files/town-of-greece-v-galloway/))

## 4. Structural Church-State Separation Failures: Documented Cases

### 4.1 Supreme Court Erosion of the Establishment Clause (2017–2025)

The Roberts Court has issued a sequence of rulings that legal scholars describe as systematically eroding the Establishment Clause while strengthening the Free Exercise Clause — the result being that government neutrality toward religion is being recharacterized as hostility:

| Case | Year | Ruling | Church-State Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Trinity Lutheran Church v. Comer* | 2017 | Religious organizations cannot be excluded from government aid programs solely because of religious status | First ruling requiring government aid flow to churches |
| *Espinoza v. Montana Dept. of Revenue* | 2020 | States that create private school voucher programs must fund religious schools alongside secular ones | Overturned Blaine Amendment-style no-aid clauses in 38 state constitutions |
| *Carson v. Makin* | 2022 | State must fund religious instruction as part of an educational aid program | Extended *Espinoza*: states now **required** to fund religious indoctrination in some contexts |
| *Kennedy v. Bremerton School District* | 2022 | Coach's midfield post-game prayers with players constituted protected personal religious expression | Overturned prior prohibition on school officials conducting prayers with students |
| *Catholic Charities Bureau v. Wisconsin LIRC* | 2025 (9-0) | Wisconsin could not deny Catholic Charities a religious tax exemption by parsing what counted as religious purpose | Expanded religious exemptions from generally applicable law |

Sources: [ACLU](https://www.aclu.org/news/religious-liberty/the-supreme-court-benches-the-separation-of-church-and-state); [Education Commission of the States](https://www.ecs.org/the-other-branch-exploring-espinoza-v-montana-department-of-revenue/); [Becket Fund](https://becketfund.org/case/catholic-charities-bureau/)

As Justice Sotomayor wrote in her *Carson* dissent: these rulings lead "us to a place where separation of church and state becomes a constitutional violation."

**Oklahoma Religious Charter School (2025):** The Supreme Court deadlocked 4-4 in *Oklahoma Statewide Virtual Charter School Board v. Drummond* over whether publicly funded religious charter schools (St. Isidore Seville Catholic Virtual School) are constitutional. The tie preserved the Oklahoma Supreme Court's ruling that such schools violate the Establishment Clause, but left the broader question unresolved for future litigation. ([Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/5/22/us-supreme-court-hits-deadlock-in-case-of-publicly-funded-religious-school); [BJC](https://bjconline.org/supreme-court-leaves-ban-on-religious-charter-schools-052325/))

### 4.2 Faith-Based Initiatives: Federal Grants to Religious Organizations

- Bush's White House Office of Faith-Based and Community Initiatives (OFBCI, established Jan. 2001) allowed religious organizations to compete directly for federal social service contracts while retaining the right to hire exclusively on religious grounds.
- By **FY2005**, more than **$2.2 billion** in competitive social service grants went to faith-based organizations in that year alone.
- Between FY2003–2005, total dollar value of federal grants to faith-based organizations **increased 21%**. ([Wikipedia / OFBCI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_House_Office_of_Faith-Based_and_Neighborhood_Partnerships))
- Critics, including Americans United for Separation of Church and State and the ACLU, argued the program violated the Establishment Clause by using taxpayer money to fund religion. ([MTSU First Amendment Encyclopedia](https://firstamendment.mtsu.edu/article/faith-based-organizations-and-government/))
- Under Bush rules: FBOs receiving direct federal grants could not use those funds for prayer or worship, but could use funds for hiring based on religion — a structural discrimination exemption for tax-funded entities. ([Wikipedia / OFBCI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_House_Office_of_Faith-Based_and_Neighborhood_Partnerships))

### 4.3 Legislative Prayer and Government Meetings

- *Town of Greece v. Galloway* (2014): The Court (5-4) upheld town board meetings opened with predominantly Christian prayer, finding the practice consistent with the legislative prayer tradition. ([National Constitution Center](https://constitutioncenter.org/the-constitution/supreme-court-case-library/town-of-greece-v-galloway))
- This creates a documented structural situation: non-Christian and non-religious citizens attending local government meetings — to participate in zoning hearings, permit applications, or public comment — are subjected to government-sponsored religious ceremonies.

### 4.4 Department of Education School Prayer Guidance Shift (2026)

- The Biden DOE's 2023 guidance (issued post-*Kennedy*) emphasized that school officials may not formally lead or promote prayer.
- The Trump DOE's February 2026 guidance de-emphasized the "separation of church and state" framework, replacing it with "neutrality among and accommodation toward all faiths." It devoted approximately one-third of its text to reinforcing parental religious rights. ([KingSpry Law](https://kingspry.com/usdoe-updates-prayer-in-school-guidance/); [JD Supra](https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/u-s-department-of-education-recasts-2439501/))
- The shift in framing — from "separation" to "accommodation" — is a documented structural change in how federal education policy interprets the Establishment Clause.

### 4.5 Religiously Motivated Legislation: Abortion as Case Study

- The PRRI (2025 survey) found approximately **one-third of Americans** qualify as adherents or sympathizers of Christian nationalism. Among Republicans, the figure rises to **56%**. ([The Humanist, citing PRRI](https://thehumanist.com/news/national/when-christian-nationalism-becomes-public-policy/))
- *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization* (2022) returned abortion regulation to states. While the Court's ruling did not invoke religion, faith-based groups filed amici arguing the Mississippi ban "offends values of religious pluralism… by imposing the view of certain faiths upon all people." ([Center for Reproductive Rights](https://reproductiverights.org/resources/faith-based-groups-amicus-brief-in-dobbs-v-jackson-womens-health/))
- Post-*Dobbs*, Indiana courts have affirmed injunctions against state abortion bans partly because they violate religious freedom for individuals whose sincere religious beliefs would direct them to seek an abortion. ([PMC / AJOG Global Reports](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11952793/)) This represents a documented policy outcome — abortion restriction — where religious motivation of the restriction is judicially acknowledged to potentially violate the religious freedom of others.

### 4.6 Title VII Ministerial Exception: Government Non-Enforcement

- Religious organizations are exempt from federal civil rights employment law under Title VII for all staff positions — not just clergy. This was expanded in the 1972 Civil Rights Act amendment to cover receptionists and groundskeepers in religious organizations.
- The "ministerial exception" provides that for key religious leaders, non-discrimination law "simply does not apply at all." This is a structural gap in civil rights law for employees of religious institutions that receive government funding. ([Center for Public Justice](https://cpjustice.org/what-we-do/institutional-religious-freedom-alliance/religious-hiring/title-vii-exemption-and-ministerial-exception/))

## 6. Key Gaps and Caveats

1. **Disclosure opacity**: Because churches are exempt from both IRS Form 990 filing and the Lobbying Disclosure Act, all spending figures for religious organizations in policy advocacy are structural underestimates. The true scale is unknowable by design.

2. **Causality vs. correlation**: Demonstrating that a specific piece of legislation passed *because* of religious lobbying rather than co-occurring religious demographics is difficult to prove directly. The documented mechanisms (funding, voter mobilization, chaplaincy, SCOTUS doctrinal shift) are structural, not always transactional.

3. **Bidirectional religious influence**: Religious organizations also lobby for positions aligned with progressive policy (the Friends Committee on National Legislation — a Quaker group — was the top-spending religious lobbyist in 2024 at $4 million, likely on peace and social justice issues). The structural advantages accrue across the ideological spectrum, though the scale of right-wing Christian mobilization operations (FFC $62M) dwarfs progressive religious lobbying.

4. **CAIR data**: The claim of $3.8M in undisclosed lobbying by CAIR-CA comes from a single 2025 report by the Network Contagion Research Institute, which has been described as having a particular political orientation. Independent verification is limited.
