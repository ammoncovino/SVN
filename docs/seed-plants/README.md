# SVN — 10 Seed Plants for the Proving Grounds

Each plant is a structural problem. Not an opinion. Not a side. A mechanism that can be tested, measured, and proven or disproven. The Alpha-Omega Lens should score these high on logic, verifiability, and scope — and low on rhetoric.

These are Biosphere-level plants (cost: 5 energy). They affect the entire system.

## PLANT 1 — Biosphere
### How Judges Get Their Power: The Structural Corruption of Judicial Selection

The United States uses three systems to select judges — presidential appointment with Senate confirmation (federal), partisan election (39 states for some courts), and merit selection commissions (33 states for some courts). Each system has a measurable failure mode.

**Federal:** Confirmation timelines have grown from ~1 month under Reagan to 6+ months today. In 2015–16, Senate Republicans blocked 100+ Obama judicial nominees, then confirmed 234 Trump judges in four years — shifting the appellate court from 39.7% to 53.6% Republican-appointee majority in a single term. At least 85% of Trump's appellate nominees were active Federalist Society members. Leonard Leo's network received a single $1.6 billion donation from Barre Seid — the largest known political advocacy donation in U.S. history — with $580M+ deployed into judicial confirmation campaigns.

**State:** Where judges face elections, their behavior measurably changes. Research shows trial judges sentence more harshly near re-election. Elected judges reverse death sentences at roughly half the rate of appointed judges. TV ad spending directly correlates with anti-defendant rulings. State supreme court race spending hit $157.3 million in 2023–24, with outside interest groups outspending candidates for the first time (54% vs. 45%).

**Accountability:** A Wall Street Journal investigation found 137 federal judges who presided over 796 cases where they owned stock in a party — ruling in favor of their financial interests in two-thirds of contested motions. Justice Thomas received $2.4M in disclosed gifts over 20 years — nearly 10x all other justices combined. Fix the Court documented 25+ missed Supreme Court recusals since 2012 with no consequences.

**The testable question:** If judicial selection were restructured around 18-year term limits (67–78% public support across polls, endorsed by scholars from the Federalist Society's own co-founder to John Roberts himself), would case outcomes become measurably less predictable by the appointing president's party?

**Sources:** [Brookings Institution](https://www.brookings.edu), [Fix the Court](https://fixthecourt.com), [Brennan Center for Justice](https://www.brennancenter.org), [Wall Street Journal investigation](https://www.wsj.com), [OpenSecrets](https://www.opensecrets.org)

## PLANT 2 — Biosphere
### The Two-Party Lock: How Structural Mechanics Prevent Problem-Solving in Congress

The United States has had two dominant parties since 1852. This is not cultural preference — it's mathematical inevitability. Duverger's Law demonstrates that single-member-district, plurality-wins elections mechanically produce two parties through strategic voting and strategic campaigning. The system doesn't fail to produce alternatives — it's structurally designed to prevent them.

**Measurable gridlock:** Congress passed an average of 443 public laws per session from 1993–2000. The 118th Congress (2023–24) passed 274 — the lowest in 40+ years. There have been 21+ government shutdowns since 1976, escalating from $700,000 in cost (1980) to $11 billion (2025). The debt ceiling has been raised 78 times since 1960.

**Safe seats eliminate accountability:** In 2024, only 37 of 435 House districts (8.5%) were decided by 5 points or less. 244 districts — 6 in 10 — were won by 25+ points. Republican-drawn maps produce competitive districts at 4% vs. 3x more under independent commissions. DW-NOMINATE ideology scores show zero ideological overlap between parties in either chamber since 2002.

**The primary trap:** Fear of primary challenge — not just voter ideology — is the dominant behavioral distortion. Members represent the ~15% of eligible voters who show up to primaries, not their full constituency.

**Structural alternatives with data:** Alaska's ranked-choice voting produced cross-party coalition results in 2022 and survived a repeal referendum 51–49% in 2024. California's top-two primary system produced an 18-point moderation effect on newly elected members, dropped uncontested primaries from 80%+ to under 20%, and reduced average winning margins by 10 points.

**The testable question:** If 10 states adopted ranked-choice or nonpartisan primary systems, would the DW-NOMINATE ideological gap narrow measurably within two election cycles?

**Sources:** [National Archives](https://www.archives.gov), [Duverger's Law political science literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duverger%27s_law), [FiveThirtyEight/538](https://fivethirtyeight.com), [New America](https://www.newamerica.org), [Alaska Division of Elections](https://www.elections.alaska.gov)

## PLANT 3 — Biosphere
### The Triple Exemption: How Religious Organizations Operate Outside Every Transparency Rule

Religious organizations in the United States hold a triple exemption that no other institutional category possesses: no Form 990 filing requirement, no Lobbying Disclosure Act obligations, and automatic 501(c)(3) tax-exempt status without IRS review. This is not a claim about religion being good or bad — it is a structural fact about accountability.

**Invisible spending:** The formally registered religious lobbying sector reported $5.14 million in 2024 (OpenSecrets). This number is structurally meaningless. The U.S. Conference of Catholic Bishops spends ~$26.7 million annually on policy activity without disclosing it as lobbying. The Faith and Freedom Coalition ran a $62 million voter mobilization program in 2024 through a 501(c)(4) dark-money structure. In July 2025, an IRS consent decree effectively neutered Johnson Amendment enforcement for religious organizations, creating a new pathway for tax-deductible political spending with no disclosure requirement.

**Doctrine overriding evidence:** The federal government spent $2.2 billion on abstinence-only sex education from 1996–2018, with $110 million still allocated in FY2024. Cochrane meta-analysis, CDC reviews, and Mathematica evaluations all found zero evidence these programs reduce teen pregnancy or STI rates. They continued because the structural funding pipeline was built on religious doctrine, not measurable outcomes.

**Active enforcement of religious tests:** In 2023, South Carolina refused to swear in an atheist poll worker who would not say "so help me God." Congressional chaplains cost ~$800,000/year; over 96% of prayers since 2000 have been Christian. The Supreme Court's trajectory from Trinity Lutheran (2017) through Carson and Kennedy (2022) to Catholic Charities Bureau (2025) has systematically expanded Free Exercise protections while treating Establishment Clause enforcement as hostility to religion.

**The testable question:** If religious organizations were required to file Form 990 disclosures (like every other nonprofit), would the documented spending on policy influence exceed the formally reported $5.14M by a factor of 10 or more?

**Sources:** [OpenSecrets](https://www.opensecrets.org), [Cochrane Reviews](https://www.cochranelibrary.com), [IRS nonprofit data](https://www.irs.gov), [Brennan Center for Justice](https://www.brennancenter.org), [Congressional Research Service](https://crsreports.congress.gov)

## PLANT 4 — Biosphere
### Property Tax Funding: The Mechanism That Guarantees Unequal Education

The U.S. public education system draws 43% of its funding from local sources, and 81% of that local funding comes from property taxes. This means the quality of a child's education is structurally determined by the property values in their zip code. This is not a policy choice being debated — it's the active funding mechanism in nearly every state.

**The funding gap is measurable:** Per-pupil spending ranges from $10,247 in Idaho to $30,012+ in New York — a roughly 3:1 ratio. Within districts, the disparity persists: Buffalo's high-poverty schools receive 26% less per pupil than wealthy schools in the same district. A $1,000 per-pupil spending increase produces a measurable +0.15 standard deviation improvement in outcomes and a 9-percentage-point graduation increase — but only in underfunded districts. In 36 states, higher total spending coincided with falling NAEP scores, because the new money went to already-funded districts.

**Outcomes by school type:** 40% of 4th graders scored below NAEP Basic in reading in 2024 — worse than any year since 2005. Private school 8th graders score ~20 points higher in reading than public school peers, but that advantage largely disappears when controlling for socioeconomic status. Charter schools overall gain +16 days reading and +6 days math vs. matched traditional public school peers (CREDO III, 2023) — but Black students in poverty gain +37 days, while students with disabilities lose 13–14 days and online charters lose 58–124 days.

**Who controls what children learn:** 45 states delegate curriculum to local districts. But Texas, California, and Florida collectively purchase ~25% of all K-12 publisher revenue, giving three state boards outsized control over national textbook content. In 2023, the Texas SBOE — with board members holding fossil-fuel industry ties — rejected evolution-accurate and climate-accurate textbooks. Eight of 22 publisher submissions were rejected. Publishers preemptively self-censored to secure adoption.

**Homeschool accountability gap:** 11 states require zero notification to homeschool. 47 states allow convicted child abusers to homeschool their children. No state requires contact with a mandatory reporter. A CRHE alumni survey found 1 in 4 homeschool graduates experienced educational neglect or abuse. 61% of documented abuse cases only came to light after death or escape.

**The testable question:** If per-pupil funding were equalized within each state (decoupled from property tax), would the NAEP score gap between the lowest and highest-spending districts narrow by more than 30% within 8 years?

**Sources:** [National Center for Education Statistics](https://nces.ed.gov), [NAEP data](https://nationsreportcard.gov), [CREDO at Stanford](https://credo.stanford.edu), [Coalition for Responsible Home Education](https://responsiblehomeschooling.org), [Texas State Board of Education records](https://tea.texas.gov)

## PLANT 5 — Forest (Texas)
### Austin Energy and the Deregulation Lie: Who Actually Pays More?

Austin Energy is city-owned. The Austin City Council sets its rates directly, with no independent board between ratepayers and elected officials — making Austin the largest U.S. city with this governance arrangement. This creates a measurable structural conflict, but it also produces measurably cheaper electricity than the deregulated alternative.

**The conflict:** Austin Energy transfers $115 million per year (11.6% of non-power-supply revenue) to the City General Fund — double the national municipal median. Council members simultaneously vote on electricity rates and on how those revenues fund their political deliverables (parks, police, libraries). Rate cases happen only every ~5 years, allowing misalignment to compound.

**The comparison that matters:** Public power utilities are on average 7% cheaper than investor-owned utilities. In Texas specifically, deregulated-market customers pay 20–30% more than Austin and San Antonio municipal customers. Over 2002–2016, the cumulative gap was $27 billion. Investor-owned utilities carry a 7–11% cost of capital vs. 4–5% for municipal utilities, plus corporate tax burden and a profit motive that structurally incentivizes capital spending (more infrastructure = more rate base = more profit).

**Winter Storm Uri (2021):** The ERCOT grid nearly collapsed entirely. Gas producers cut output before utilities cut power, then reaped an $11.1 billion windfall in five days. ERCOT kept prices at $9,000/MWh for ~32 hours after the shortage ended, generating ~$16 billion in excess charges that were never repriced. Costs were socialized through $8.6 billion in securitization bonds paid by ratepayers. Brazos Electric's bond extends to 2049. Consumers absorbed damages. Suppliers and pipeline operators kept profits.

**Grid investment paralysis:** No single authority can build what the grid needs. Transmission projects require 10–15 years from concept to energization, sequential state-by-state siting approval, NEPA review averaging 6.5 years, and FERC backstop authority that only triggers after states fail. 90% of transmission investment since 1996 was reactive reliability spending — not forward-looking planning. The interconnection queue holds 2,300–2,600 GW of proposed capacity (roughly the entire existing U.S. fleet), 95%+ zero-carbon. Median wait: 5 years. Only 19% of projects that applied ever get built.

**The testable question:** If ERCOT repriced the 32 hours of post-shortage $9,000/MWh charges at the actual supply-demand clearing price, how much of the $16 billion in excess charges would have been eliminated — and would any retail provider have gone bankrupt?

**Sources:** [Austin Energy financial reports](https://austinenergy.com), [ERCOT market data](https://www.ercot.com), [American Public Power Association](https://www.publicpower.org), [Federal Energy Regulatory Commission](https://www.ferc.gov), [Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory interconnection queue studies](https://emp.lbl.gov)

## PLANT 6 — Forest (Texas)
### Why I-35 Takes 45 Years: The Structural Mechanics of TxDOT Paralysis

The I-35 Capital Express Central project in Austin began planning in the late 1980s. Construction started in October 2024. Estimated completion: 2032–2033. That's roughly 45 years from concept to completion for 8 miles of highway, at a cost of $4.5 billion ($562 million per mile). Cap costs rose 61% in a single year.

This is not incompetence. It's the structural output of a system with six mandatory sequential phases, each with its own approval chain.

**The pipeline:** TxDOT projects move through Planning → Programming → Preliminary Engineering → Final Design → Letting → Construction. FHWA states that major highway projects take 9–19 years from concept to construction. NEPA environmental impact statements for highways average 6.7–7.37 years — nearly double the government-wide median.

**MoPac South:** Environmental study initiated 2013. Draft Environmental Assessment finally released February 2026 — 13+ years of environmental review to add 8 miles of express lanes. A lawsuit halted the process for 2+ years mid-study.

**SH-130:** Built relatively quickly (3.5 years construction) under a public-private partnership. But traffic projections were catastrophically wrong. The project filed Chapter 11 bankruptcy in 2016 on $1.3 billion in debt, exited with $260 million in new debt, and zero accountability to taxpayers for the failed projections.

**Federal cost inflators:** Davis-Bacon wage requirements inflate highway construction labor costs 22–34% above market rates, adding 8–14% to total project cost. Buy America provisions (expanded in 2021) add procurement complexity and supplier risk. These are federal mandates attached to federal dollars — Texas cannot opt out without forfeiting funding.

**Funding decay:** The Texas gas tax (20¢/gallon) was last raised in 1991. The federal gas tax (18.4¢/gallon) was last raised in 1993. Both have lost roughly half their real purchasing power. Prop 1 (2014) diverts oil and gas severance taxes — volatile and temporary. Prop 7 (2015) diverts sales tax — more stable at $3.2 billion/year but restricted to non-toll roads with sunset clauses.

**International comparison:** China went from zero freeways in 1997 to surpassing the total U.S. Interstate network by 2014 — 17 years — adding 4,000–5,000 miles per year. The U.S. adds under 800. U.S. spending per highway project is 3x higher than comparable countries. Spain built 9,500+ km of motorways in 20 years with competitive procurement and EU co-funding.

**The testable question:** If NEPA review for highway projects were capped at 2 years (as the FAST Act attempted), and Davis-Bacon waivers were granted for projects under $500M, would the average Texas highway project timeline drop below 10 years — and would safety or environmental outcomes measurably worsen?

**Sources:** [TxDOT project data](https://www.txdot.gov), [Federal Highway Administration](https://www.fhwa.dot.gov), [NEPA.gov completion time data](https://ceq.doe.gov), [Congressional Research Service on Davis-Bacon](https://crsreports.congress.gov), [International Transport Forum/OECD](https://www.itf-oecd.org)

## PLANT 7 — Biosphere
### The Litigation Machine: How Tort Law Replaced Personal Responsibility With a Revenue Model

If someone slips on your property in the United States, the legal system structurally assumes you may owe them money — regardless of whether they were being careless. Under comparative negligence (now the rule in 46 states), a plaintiff who is 49% at fault can still recover 51% of damages. Only 4 jurisdictions still use pure contributory negligence, where any plaintiff fault bars recovery entirely.

This isn't about fairness. It's about the structural economics of the system that grew around these rules.

**The contingency fee engine:** Attorneys take personal injury cases at 33.3–40% of the recovery with zero upfront cost to the plaintiff. This creates a structural incentive to file — the plaintiff risks nothing, the attorney evaluates cases by expected settlement value, and "nuisance value" settlements ($5,000–$15,000) are cheaper for defendants to pay than to fight. Represented plaintiffs receive an average of $77,600 vs. $17,600 for unrepresented — a 4.4x multiplier that is itself the marketing pitch for the system.

**Third-party litigation funding** has grown into a $15–19 billion market projected to reach $31 billion — hedge funds and investors bankrolling lawsuits for a percentage of the recovery. The plaintiff may not even know who is financing their case.

**The cost:** The U.S. tort system costs $529 billion annually — 2.1% of GDP, 2.6x the Eurozone rate. 43% of that cost is overhead (attorney fees, administrative costs), not compensation to injured parties. General liability insurance averages $810/year per business. Homeowner insurance premiums rose 40.4% from 2019–2024. Pre-reform Florida generated 76% of all U.S. homeowner litigation despite holding only 8% of claims.

**Texas tort reform (2003) — the data:** Dallas County filings dropped from 1,108 to 142 in Year 1 (87% reduction). Medical malpractice premiums fell 30–55%. Physician growth rate increased from 0.854% to 1.45% per year (+69%). But — and this is the critical finding — Silver et al. found no measurable reduction in healthcare costs. Medicare spending in Texas rose 1–2% faster than the national average post-reform.

**The testable question:** If the U.S. adopted the English "loser pays" rule (where the losing party pays the winner's legal costs), would nuisance-value filings decrease by more than 50% — and would legitimate injury compensation amounts increase, decrease, or remain flat? Florida tested this from 1980–1985: the drop rate rose from 44% to 54% (frivolous cases filtered out), average settlements rose from $73,786 to $94,489, and trial awards tripled.

**Sources:** [Institute for Legal Reform](https://instituteforlegalreform.com), [Triple-I/CAS actuarial studies](https://www.iii.org), [Texas Department of Insurance data](https://www.tdi.texas.gov), [Silver et al. academic study on Texas tort reform](https://scholarship.law.duke.edu), [Bloomberg Law comparative fault database](https://www.bloomberglaw.com)

## PLANT 8 — Biosphere
### Blacked-Out Pages: The Structural Mechanics of Government Secrecy

The U.S. government classifies approximately 49 million pieces of information per year. The entire classification system — who can classify, at what level, for how long — runs on presidential executive orders, not legislation. There is no statute. Each administration can reshape the rules unilaterally.

**How classification actually works:** Executive Order 13526 establishes three levels (Confidential, Secret, Top Secret) and nine exemption categories. In FY2017, for every one original classification decision, there were 838 derivative classifications — bureaucrats copying the secrecy label forward without evaluating whether it still applies. The ISOO Director has warned that most digital classified records "will never be reviewed for declassification." Experts estimate 50–90% of classified material is over-classified. ISOO inspections find a 34% error rate. 70% of Security Classification Guides were deficient in FY2023.

**The Epstein files:** Between December 2025 and March 2026, 3.5 million+ pages were released under the Epstein Transparency Act. Five categories of redaction were permitted. The verified violations tell the story: software-bypassable redactions covered trafficking ring members (not victims), politically inconvenient emails were improperly withheld, and actual victim-identifying information that should have been redacted was left exposed. Files were removed from public access after release. No enforcement consequences followed.

**FOIA is structurally broken:** In FY2024, agencies received 1.5 million requests (+25% over prior year) and backlogged 267,056 (+33%). The statutory deadline is 20 business days. The average simple request took 44 days. The full grant rate hit an all-time low of 12.11% — down from 38% in 2010. Two exemptions — (b)(6) personal privacy and (b)(7)(C) law enforcement privacy — account for 63% of all exemptions cited.

**No accountability for political redactions:** There is no criminal statute for over-classification. Qualified immunity shields officials. Inspectors General have no enforcement power. When political controversies emerge from wrongful classification, litigation takes years — the Ukraine OMB withholding case took 3.5 years to reach a partial court order. By then, the political moment has passed.

**Congressional oversight is theater:** Congress has subpoena power, but enforcement is self-defeating. Criminal contempt requires the DOJ to prosecute itself. Civil enforcement takes years. Inherent contempt (Congress jailing witnesses) is legally available but dormant since 1935. Unilateral declassification power exists on paper but has never been exercised against presidential opposition.

**The testable question:** If a mandatory declassification review were required every 10 years (rather than 25) with a presumption of release, and if over-classification by more than one standard deviation from ISOO audit standards triggered automatic declassification, would the 49-million-per-year classification volume decrease — and would any documented national security breach result?

**Sources:** [Information Security Oversight Office (ISOO)](https://www.archives.gov/isoo), [FOIA.gov](https://www.foia.gov), [National Security Archive](https://nsarchive.gwu.edu), [Epstein Transparency Act release data](https://www.congress.gov), [Congressional Research Service](https://crsreports.congress.gov)

## PLANT 9 — Biosphere
### Rhetoric as Governance: How the System Runs on Persuasion Instead of Proof

The filibuster, the earmark, the executive order, the campaign promise, the congressional hearing — each is a structural mechanism that allows rhetoric to function as governance. Not as a side effect. As the design.

**The filibuster:** From 1917 to 1970, the Senate filed ~60 total cloture motions. The 117th Congress alone filed 336. Total historical filings now exceed 3,131. The "silent filibuster" means a senator doesn't even have to speak — the threat of rhetoric is the operative weapon. The result: any legislation needs 60 votes to pass in a body where 51 is the majority.

**Earmarks:** Must-pass bills serve as vehicles for unrelated provisions. From 1991–2009, $290 billion in earmarks were attached to legislation. In FY2023 alone: $26.1 billion. The Bridge to Nowhere ($398 million for 50 users), a $4 million sewer for a 98-person Alaskan city, Hurricane Sandy relief packed with unrelated riders. Each justified with rhetoric about local needs — none required to demonstrate structural necessity.

**Campaign promises vs. voting records:** PolitiFact tracking shows Obama broke 23% of campaign promises, Biden broke 34%, and Trump's first term broke 53%. Academic research (Ganslmeier, 2025) explains why this doesn't matter electorally: promises function as one-time vote purchases with no lasting loyalty consequence. The rhetoric works, so it continues.

**The revolving door:** Lobbying spending tripled from 1998 to $4.5 billion in 2024. In 2025, 866 former Hill staffers transitioned to lobbying firms — a 60-year record. When Citigroup's lobbyists wrote 70 of 85 lines of a financial regulation bill, the mechanism was visible: rhetoric about "free markets" and "reducing regulatory burden" translating directly into legislative text authored by the regulated industry. ALEC's model legislation pipeline produced 2,900 bill introductions and 600+ enacted laws between 2010–2018.

**Executive orders as rhetoric:** Biden issued executive orders that Trump reversed within days of taking office — a 41% reversal rate. A Cambridge Core study shows ~75% of executive orders are never formally revoked, but partisan-change reversals are systematic. If an executive order can be undone by the next president signing a different piece of paper, its structural purpose is messaging — not governance.

**Congressional hearings:** Bills have a 1.4% enactment rate. Only 19% of House hearings involve genuine executive oversight. 45% of oversight hearings involve no actual agency scrutiny. Testimony changes votes 6% of the time. An APSR study found hearings produce effects "an order of magnitude smaller" than what OMB considers a meaningful policy reduction.

**The testable question:** If the Senate eliminated the silent filibuster (requiring actual continuous floor speech), and if all earmarks required a published cost-benefit analysis reviewed by GAO before attachment, would the legislative output of Congress measurably increase in both volume and structural quality?

**Sources:** [U.S. Senate cloture data](https://www.senate.gov), [OpenSecrets lobbying data](https://www.opensecrets.org), [PolitiFact](https://www.politifact.com), [GovTrack](https://www.govtrack.us), [American Political Science Review](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/american-political-science-review)

## PLANT 10 — Biosphere
### The Responsibility Inversion: How the System Structurally Rewards Not Taking Responsibility

The U.S. legal, insurance, and social welfare systems have a measurable structural tendency: they transfer responsibility from individuals to institutions, and from institutions to taxpayers. This is not a moral claim. It's an observable mechanical pattern with data.

**Legal structure:** 46 states now use comparative negligence, where a plaintiff who is 49% at fault can still recover 51% of damages. The assumption-of-risk doctrine — where a person who voluntarily takes a risk cannot sue when that risk materializes — has been systematically eroded. The duty-to-warn framework in product liability replaced individual consumer judgment with manufacturer obligations: if a company doesn't warn you that coffee is hot, the system says they failed — not you.

**Insurance moral hazard:** When consequences are distributed across a risk pool, individuals face reduced incentive to avoid harm. This is not theory — it's actuarial data. Workers' compensation fraud costs $34–44 billion per year. Disability misrepresentation rates are 8.9% in insurance industry monitoring.

**The welfare cliff:** 21.8% of benefit recipients report taking at least one cliff-related action — refusing hours, turning down promotions, declining job offers — specifically to preserve eligibility. Effective marginal tax rates for low-income workers range from 17% to 65% per additional dollar earned. SSI asset limits have been frozen at $2,000 since 1989. SSDI enrollment grew 312% from 1970–2013, with NBER research confirming recession-period entrants are measurably healthier than baseline — evidence that enrollment responds to economic conditions, not just disability prevalence.

**Corporate vs. individual accountability:** DOJ corporate prosecutions fell from 304 (2000) to 90 (2021). In FY2024, only 44 of 4,332 white-collar prosecutions targeted corporations (1%). Of those, 76% of prosecuted companies had under 50 employees. 71% of companies receiving leniency deals had 5,000+ employees. The larger you are, the less responsibility the system assigns you.

**Cultural comparison:** Singapore's Central Provident Fund requires citizens to save for their own housing, healthcare, and retirement — driving homeownership from 30% to 90% with minimal welfare cliff effects. Japan's liability system costs less per capita than any other OECD-comparable nation. Both systems structurally assume individuals are responsible for their own outcomes. Both measurably outperform the U.S. on the metrics they target.

**The testable question:** If benefit phase-outs were graduated (12 states are piloting this, and the Upward Mobility Act was introduced in 2026), and if corporate deferred prosecution agreements required individual officer accountability as a condition, would workforce participation among benefit recipients increase measurably — and would corporate recidivism rates decrease?

**Sources:** [NBER disability enrollment research](https://www.nber.org), [Department of Justice prosecution statistics](https://www.justice.gov), [Congressional Budget Office marginal tax rate analysis](https://www.cbo.gov), [National Insurance Crime Bureau](https://www.nicb.org), [Singapore CPF Board](https://www.cpf.gov.sg)

## HOW TO USE THESE PLANTS

These are starter problems. Each one is planted at the level where it operates (8 Biosphere, 2 Forest). Each ends with a falsifiable, testable question — not an answer.

The Proving Grounds doesn't solve these problems by declaring an opinion the winner. It solves them by:

1. **Planting** the structural problem with verifiable data
2. **Joining** with additional evidence, corrections, or structural expansions
3. **Consuming** any contribution that relies on rhetoric instead of logic — the Alpha-Omega Lens identifies it, the user proves it
4. **Composting** arguments that can't survive scrutiny — their energy returns to the ecosystem for someone else to use better

No one gets to win by being louder. No one gets to hide behind "that's just my opinion." The lens sees through it. The energy mechanics enforce it.

This is what the SVN was built for.
