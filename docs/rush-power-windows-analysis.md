# Rush — *Power Windows* (1985): Deep Systems Analysis
### Focus Songs: "Marathon" and "Territories"

---

## 1. THE ALBUM: *POWER WINDOWS* AS A CONCEPT

### What "Power Windows" Means

The title operates on three simultaneous levels:

1. **Literal/consumer**: "Power windows" refers to electrically operated car windows — in the mid-1980s, a premium feature that replaced manual hand-cranking. The album artwork literalizes the joke: a man in a suit points a TV remote control at a wooden window in a wall, surrounded by vintage television sets. The image signals that this is about the remote control of things — the managed gaze, the mediated view.

2. **Metaphorical/conceptual**: According to Geddy Lee in interview, each song on the album is meant to be a *"window into power"* — a different aperture onto the concept of power in its various forms. The album is a taxonomy of power: economic, military, territorial, personal, emotional, natural. The title means: these songs let you look through the glass at power as it actually operates.

3. **Structural/ironic**: A window gives you a view — but it is framed, bounded, filtered. You can see through it but not touch what's on the other side. The cover image (remote pointed at a window) suggests the illusion of control. You can aim your remote at the world, but you cannot control what the world does. Power is both available and out of reach.

The album was released October 11, 1985. Neil Peart wrote the lyrics while living in a farmhouse in Elora, Ontario, while Lifeson and Lee developed music in an adjacent barn studio. The full record was produced with Peter Collins and recorded across five studios including The Manor (England), SARM East (London), and AIR Studios (Montserrat).

### The Album's Organizing Thesis

*Power Windows* is not a concept album in the strict narrative sense, but it is a *thematic* album with unusual coherence. Peart maps eight distinct types of power across eight tracks:

| Track | Song | Type of Power |
|-------|------|---------------|
| 1 | The Big Money | Economic / systemic capital |
| 2 | Grand Designs | Creative / artistic — individual vs. conformity |
| 3 | Manhattan Project | Technological / military — power that exceeds its makers |
| 4 | Marathon | Personal / internal — willpower, endurance, time |
| 5 | Territories | Territorial / nationalistic — group identity as weapon |
| 6 | Middletown Dreams | The power of aspiration — dreams as resistance to stasis |
| 7 | Emotion Detector | Emotional power — vulnerability, exposure, the cost of openness |
| 8 | Mystic Rhythms | Natural / cosmic — power beyond understanding or control |

Critic Brad Birzer (Progarchy) frames the album's meta-theme as **"excellence against conformity"** — each type of power examined from the perspective of whether it serves human flourishing or destroys it. This places *Power Windows* in a sequence of Rush albums (Moving Pictures → Signals → Grace Under Pressure → Power Windows) that tracks the lifecycle of individual integrity: assertion, cost, recovery, and finally the mature confrontation with external systems.

### What the Song Is About: Neil Peart in His Own Words

Peart was explicit about the song's genesis and intent:

> *"The song is about the triumph of time and a kind of message to myself, because I think life is too short for all the things that I want to do. There's a self-admonition saying that life is long enough. You can do a lot, just don't burn yourself out too fast trying to do everything at once. 'Marathon' is a song about individual goals and trying to achieve them. And it's also about the old Chinese proverb: 'The journey of a thousand miles begins with one step.'"*  
> — Neil Peart, *Canadian Composer*, quoted in *Merely Players*

The primary literary inspiration was Ernest Hemingway's adopted motto — taken from a statue of one of Napoleon's generals — **"Il faut d'abord durer"**: "First, one must last." Peart's shortened version: *"At first, to last."* This became the conceptual spine of the song. The line "First you've got to last" is a direct encoding of this Napoleonic military maxim applied to the long game of life.

*Source: [Rush Vault — Marathon](https://rushvault.com/2011/02/07/marathon/), [Every Rush Song analysis](https://everyrushsong.com/analysis-of-the-rush-song-marathon/)*

### Structural Themes

#### 1. The Anti-Sprint Principle
The song opens with a fundamental inversion of competitive logic: *"It's not how fast you can go / The force goes into the flow."* This is not a celebration of passive drifting — it is a precise statement about energy allocation. Force that goes into *speed* dissipates quickly. Force that enters *flow* — the efficient, sustainable state — compounds over time. The song describes a physics of sustained effort.

Critics Carol Selby Price and Robert Price (*Mystic Rhythms*) make the structural logic explicit: *"The song rejects the 'live fast, die early' model of life... Life is a long-distance run, and if you end it fast, it's only because you paced yourself poorly and dropped out somewhere along the way."*

#### 2. "The Peak Is Never Passed"
This is the song's most counterintuitive and philosophically loaded line. In conventional achievement logic, the peak is the goal — you climb toward it, reach it, and the journey ends. Peart inverts this. The peak is never passed because:

- There is always another peak beyond the one you reached
- The journey itself is the condition of meaning, not the destination
- Each "peak" is actually a ridge — behind it is more terrain

The chorus extends this into a comment on glory: *"One moment's high, and glory rolls on by / Like a streak of lightning that flashes and fades in the summer sky."* Glory is real but transient. The trap is to chase the flash rather than building the long-run capacity. The song is warning against optimization for peak moments at the expense of sustained trajectory.

#### 3. The "Heartbreak Hill" Mechanism
*"It's a test of ultimate will / The heartbreak climb uphill."* "Heartbreak Hill" is a real term from marathon running — the brutal incline near mile 20 of the Boston Marathon, where runners who went out too fast hit their physiological wall. It is structurally the place where poor pacing reveals itself. Peart uses this as a metaphor for the inflection point in any long-term endeavor where early overextension becomes catastrophic failure.

The song's solution: *"If you pick up the beat / You can forget about the heat."* Increase cadence (not necessarily speed) at the point of maximum resistance. Rhythm — the regular beat — overrides pain. This is a systems insight: it is not force that gets you through the hard passage, it is *pattern maintenance*.

#### 4. Permission to Rest Without Quitting
*"Your meters may overload / You can rest at the side of the road / You can miss a stride / But nobody gets a free ride."* This is the song's most nuanced section and often overlooked. Peart allows for rest — systems can overload — but not for exemption. Rest is legitimate maintenance. A free ride (the expectation of progress without effort) is not.

This creates a distinction between *recovery* (necessary, sanctioned) and *bypass* (a fantasy, rejected). The long-game system requires periodic withdrawal but never permanent exit from the effort.

#### 5. "More Than Just" — The Negative Definition
Verses 1, 2, and 3 are structured around a repeated "more than just X" formulation:
- More than just survival / More than just a flash / More than just a dotted line / More than just a dash
- More than just blind ambition / More than just simple greed / More than just a finish line
- More than high performance / More than just a spark / More than just the bottom line / Or a lucky shot in the dark

This rhetorical structure works by negation — defining what the long game is NOT. Not mere survival. Not ambition or greed. Not bottom-line performance metrics. Not luck. The song strips away motivations that are either too small (survival, flash) or too corrupted (greed, ambition divorced from meaning) or too accidental (lucky shot). What remains? *"Must feed this burning need / In the long run."* The motivating force is intrinsic — a burning need that sustains itself, not external reward or competitive position.

#### 6. The Historical Undertow: Battle of Marathon (490 BC)
Progarchy's analysis notes that "Marathon" carries a secondary historical resonance: the Battle of Marathon in 490 BC, where Athenian soldiers defeated the Persian invasion force against significant odds — an underdog force outlasting a superior one through discipline and will. The legendary run of the messenger Pheidippides to announce the Greek victory — a run so extreme he died after delivering it — is the origin of the marathon race itself. The song's title thus carries the weight of historical precedent: the marathon as *the original test of whether a smaller, disciplined force could outlast a larger one.* Endurance as a military and civilizational virtue, not just an athletic one.

*Source: [Progarchy analysis](https://progarchy.com/2013/12/14/power-windows-rush-and-excellence-against-conformity/)*

### What the Song Is About: Neil Peart in His Own Words

> *"The title comes from an area around Hong Kong called The New Territories. I was struck by the sound of that word, and the territorial instinct. And what with the Northwest Territories being part of Canada, it was just the right sort of word to describe what I was after. As for the opening line about the Middle Kingdom, that's still what China calls itself today. The reason for the Middle Kingdom is because it's a middle between Heaven and Earth. In other words, it's slightly below Heaven — but still above everybody on Earth. Some people look at patriotism or nationalism as being the next best thing to loyalty to your family. I don't buy it."*  
> — Neil Peart, *Canadian Composer*, quoted in *Merely Players*

The song was directly inspired by Rush's tour of Japan in 1984 and Peart's subsequent cycling trip through China in 1985. The line "Better people — better food — and better beer" is a direct quote-type parody of the kinds of things tourists (and invaders) say about comparing their home culture to wherever they've traveled — heard by Peart from fellow travelers during the Japan trip.

Peart's further critique, from his book *Roadshow*: *"Like religion, that kind of patriotism definitely causes a lot of trouble."* He specifically observed the irony of immigrants who left their home countries (often due to hardship) and then spent decades insisting their homeland was superior — *"like living with someone who is always talking about a former lover."*

*Source: [Rush Vault — Territories](https://rushvault.com/2011/02/07/territories/), [Blogtopus Blues / Neil Peart interview](https://octopusmediaink.com/surviving-with-rush/)*

### Structural Themes

#### 1. The Universal Game
The song opens by identifying a universal cognitive architecture: every human group, regardless of location or culture, constructs a self-concept that places itself closer to the divine or ideal than all others. China calls itself the "Middle Kingdom" — literally between Heaven and Earth, below heaven but above all earthly others. But Peart's move is immediate: *"We all figure that our homes are set above / Other people than the ones we know and love."*

This is not a critique of China specifically. It is a structural observation: the Middle Kingdom is everyone's psychological home address. The opening verse names the mechanism before naming its consequences. Every place that has a name plays "the same territorial game."

#### 2. "The Eyeglass in Reverse" — The Epistemological Problem
The chorus contains the song's sharpest analytical instrument: *"The whole wide world / An endless universe / Yet we keep looking through / The eyeglass in reverse."*

An eyeglass (telescope or monocle) in reverse magnifies distance instead of reducing it. It makes far things look farther, small things smaller. Looking through the eyeglass in reverse means: the larger your environment, the smaller your actual field of view — because you are using your own position as the lens. The instrument that should open the world is instead being used to confirm that everything outside your circle is distant and inferior.

This is a precise structural critique of parochialism as an epistemological failure: the problem is not that people are evil; it is that they are using the wrong orientation for their cognitive instrument. The technology of perception is inverted.

#### 3. Machines vs. People
*"Don't feed the people / But we feed the machines."* This line operates at multiple scales simultaneously:
- Military machines (armies, weapons systems, propaganda apparatus) consume resources that could feed populations
- Ideological machines (nationalism, religion, party systems) require constant feeding with human loyalty and sacrifice
- Economic machines that sustain the territorial project are maintained at the cost of the humans they nominally serve

The parallel with "Marathon" is significant: both songs distinguish between the mechanism (the machine, the race) and the animating need (the burning need, the actual people). Both songs suggest that systems can become inverted — consuming their own purpose.

#### 4. The Circular Trap
*"In different circles / We keep holding our ground / Indifferent circles / We keep spinning round and round."*

The wordplay here is precise: "different circles" and "indifferent circles." We are in different groups (circles), but those groups are *indifferent* to one another — and the circles themselves are the problem. Circles close. They spin in place. "Holding our ground" is simultaneously a military image (defending territory) and a description of stasis. You can hold your ground and go nowhere — and that is exactly what happens.

The "spinning round and round" image creates a contrast with "Marathon": in that song, the model is linear movement with pacing and direction. In "Territories," the model is circular movement with no forward progress — territorial games produce gyration, not trajectory.

#### 5. The Invader's Irony
*"We see so many tribes — overrun and undermined / While their invaders dream of lands they've left behind."*

This is the song's most structurally devastating observation. The invader — the person who has conquered or colonized another people's territory — is *still dreaming of home*. The territory seized is never Eden. The colonizer carries with them the psychological territory they left, and measures every new place against it. This is the territorial instinct consuming itself: you conquer outward while remaining mentally trapped inward. The gain is material and the loss is existential.

*"Better people — better food — and better beer / Why move around the world when Eden was so near?"* — The irony cuts both ways: the invader left Eden to conquer somewhere else, and now invents nostalgic superiority about the place they abandoned. And the conquered people had what the invader was looking for all along.

#### 6. The Leadership / Follower Mechanism
*"The bosses get talking so tough / And if that wasn't evil enough / We get the drunken and passionate pride / Of the citizens along for the ride."*

This is a systems-level critique of how territorial conflict is manufactured and sustained. The mechanism has two components:

1. **Elite rhetoric** ("the bosses get talking so tough") — leaders who generate the narrative of territorial threat, superiority, or destiny
2. **Mass affect** ("drunken and passionate pride of the citizens along for the ride") — the population that receives this rhetoric and amplifies it through emotion

Neither component alone could sustain territorial violence. The bosses' tough talk needs fuel. The citizens' drunken pride needs a target and a permission structure. Together they form a feedback loop: elite rhetoric activates mass emotion, mass emotion legitimizes elite action. The citizens are "along for the ride" — they believe themselves to be participants in an enterprise of their own making, but the vehicle is being driven by others.

This is a model of how *rhetoric* becomes *power* — and why Peart distinguishes "the bosses" from "the citizens" rather than treating nationalism as a uniform phenomenon.

#### 7. "A Change of Accent or the Colour of Your Shirt"
*"They shoot without shame / In the name of a piece of dirt / For a change of accent / Or the colour of your shirt."*

The extraordinary compression here: "a piece of dirt" reduces territorial claims to their material minimum (land is just soil). "A change of accent" reduces national/cultural difference to its most arbitrary marker — the accidents of where you were born and raised. "The colour of your shirt" reduces political or tribal identity to its most visible and arbitrary marker — a garment.

The violence is real and lethal. The markers triggering it are arbitrary. This is the song's verdict on territorial conflict: it kills for distinctions that are superficial while invoking stakes that are cosmic.

#### 8. The Final Turn: Citizenship as Alternative System
*"Better the pride that resides / In a citizen of the world / Than the pride that divides / When a colourful rag is unfurled."*

The song doesn't end with pure negation — it proposes an alternative organizational identity. The "citizen of the world" is not someone without pride or identity, but someone whose pride is organized differently: around shared humanity rather than competitive membership. The flag ("colourful rag") — stripped of its ceremonial reverence — is reduced to what it literally is: dyed cloth. The pride it symbolizes is "the pride that divides" — structurally defined by what it separates, not what it creates.

Robert Telleria (in *Merely Players*) connects this explicitly to Rush's earlier 2112 with its lyric "let the banners be unfurled" — here Peart is in direct dialogue with his own earlier work, which romanticized the revolutionary banner. By 1985, the critique has sharpened: *all* banners divide. The citizen of the world cannot carry one.

## 4. THE TWO SONGS IN DIALOGUE: STRUCTURAL PARALLELS

Read side-by-side, "Marathon" and "Territories" form a complementary system:

### Parallel 1: Energy Allocation
- **Marathon**: "The force goes into the flow" — energy channeled efficiently in the *right* system creates compound returns
- **Territories**: "Don't feed the people but we feed the machines" — energy channeled into the *wrong* system is consumed without return

Both songs are about where energy goes and what it produces. Marathon is the positive case; Territories is the negative case.

### Parallel 2: Circular vs. Linear Motion
- **Marathon**: The peak is never passed — perpetual forward trajectory; the long run has no terminal point
- **Territories**: Indifferent circles, spinning round and round — circular motion; territorial systems produce gyration without advance

### Parallel 3: The Pacing Problem
- **Marathon**: Don't burn out too fast; the danger is misallocating speed to the short term
- **Territories**: The invaders dream of lands they've left behind — stuck in the past, unable to move forward because of what they're defending

### Parallel 4: The Two Prides
- **Marathon**: The "burning need" that feeds long-term effort is intrinsic, value-based, not greed or ambition
- **Territories**: "Better the pride that resides in a citizen of the world" — pride organized around shared value vs. pride organized around competitive boundary

### Parallel 5: The Cost of Distraction
- **Marathon**: "Something always fires the light that gets in your eyes" — the bright distraction that pulls you off course
- **Territories**: The territorial game pulls entire societies off course from what they nominally want; the citizens are "along for the ride," not driving toward their actual interests

### Structural Synthesis
Both songs describe **system design problems**: in Marathon, the question is how to design a *personal* system that sustains effort over time without burning out. In Territories, the question is what happens when *collective* systems are designed around the wrong inputs (territorial pride, boss-driven rhetoric) and produce the wrong outputs (circular conflict, inverted resource allocation).

Marathon is the individual architecture of the long game.  
Territories is the failure architecture of the short game at civilizational scale.

Together they suggest that the same principle — force into flow, endurance over flash, the citizen of the world over the flag-bearer — operates at every scale from individual ambition to international relations.

## 5. OTHER SONGS ON THE ALBUM — RELATED THEMES

### "Grand Designs" (Track 2)
**What it's about**: A critique of conformity, image-over-substance culture, and the suppression of genuine creative intelligence by institutional mediocrity.

**Key lyrics and mechanisms**:
- *"So much style without substance / So much stuff without style / It's hard to recognize the real thing"* — the market for surface appearance crowds out the real thing; the signal-to-noise problem in systems where performance is visible and quality is not
- *"Like a rare and precious metal beneath a ton of rock / It takes some time and trouble to separate from the stock"* — the extraction metaphor: genuine value requires excavation; it is not immediately recognizable, and the mass production scheme (rock, stock) actively conceals it
- *"Life in two dimensions is a mass production scheme"* — a dimensional critique: reduction to two dimensions (surface + visible metrics) destroys the depth that creates actual value
- *"So much poison in power, the principles get left out / So much mind on the matter, the spirit gets forgotten about"* — the systemic inversion at power's core: as power scales up, it evacuates the principles that justified its formation

**Literary source**: Drawn from John Dos Passos's "District of Columbia" trilogy (1949), specifically as a critique of FDR's New Deal — an ambitious scheme that mistook scale for quality and generated bureaucratic conformity in the name of transformation. Peart also references T.S. Eliot's *The Hollow Men* (1925) — the people stuffed with rhetoric who lack interior substance.

**Systems connection**: Grand Designs maps the lifecycle of institutional power: starts with creative intent, ends with hollow compliance. "The principles get left out" is what happens to value systems when they encounter scale. It directly anticipates the "bosses" in Territories — who are themselves operating in two dimensions (tough talk, flag-waving) divorced from the three-dimensional reality of the people they govern.

*Source: [Rush Vault — Grand Designs](https://rushvault.com/2011/02/07/grand-designs/), [Progarchy](https://progarchy.com/2013/12/14/power-windows-rush-and-excellence-against-conformity/)*

---

### "The Big Money" (Track 1)
**What it's about**: A multivalent portrait of capital as a systemic force — capable of both extraordinary good and structural violence, simultaneously neutral and corrupting.

**Key mechanisms**:
- *"Big money pull a million strings / Big money hold the prize / Big money weave a mighty web / Big money draw the flies"* — capital as an attracting force that creates its own field; the web and the flies suggest that the prey (people) are drawn by what the system makes desirable
- *"Sometimes building you a stairway / Lock you underground"* — capital creates both access and confinement; the same system that provides opportunity builds the cage
- *"It's that old time religion / It's the kingdom they would rule"* — money as a faith system; the territorial and religious parallels are explicit; money is another "Middle Kingdom" claiming divine proximity
- *"Big money got a heavy hand / Big money take control / Big money got a mean streak / Big money got no soul"* — the system lacks the intrinsic motivation that Marathon identifies as essential; it operates on metrics, not meaning

**Neil Peart**: *"I didn't want the voice of the song to be totally in the voice of a cynical, anti-corporate reactionary, because things like the Ford Foundation do accomplish a lot of good... the church and worthy events like Live Aid are big money too."* The song is not a simple anti-capitalist screed but a systems analysis: capital amplifies human intent, good or bad.

*Source: [Rush Vault — The Big Money](https://rushvault.com/2011/02/07/the-big-money/)*

---

### "Manhattan Project" (Track 3)
**What it's about**: A precise, deliberately neutral narrative of the development of the first atomic weapon — the most extreme case on the album of a system exceeding the control of its creators.

**Systems theme**: Technology as a power that, once released, cannot be recalled. The song was notably difficult for Peart to write because he had to resist the pull of taking a moral position and instead act as an objective witness — exactly what "Territories" demands of its listener. The bomb is the ultimate territorial weapon: it makes land uninhabitable in the act of claiming it.

*Source: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_Windows_(album))*

---

### "Mystic Rhythms" (Track 8 — Closing Track)
**What it's about**: The final track shifts to the register of wonder at the systems we cannot comprehend or control — natural rhythms, cosmic forces, the unknown patterns that govern life beneath our awareness.

**Key mechanism**: *"The more we think we know about / The greater the unknown / We suspend our disbelief / And we are not alone."* This is the album's final position: after examining all the human-made systems of power (money, territory, technology, conformity), the album ends by returning to the power that predates and exceeds all of them. Mystic Rhythms is what you encounter when you have "lasted" long enough (Marathon) and released the territorial claim (Territories) — the larger patterns that were always there.

**Connection**: The Stoicism embedded throughout Peart's work finds its clearest expression here. Stoic philosophy distinguishes between what is and is not within one's control. All the songs on the album identify what *isn't* within control (the territorial game, the big money, the nuclear chain reaction) and what *is* (personal endurance, the choice to be a citizen of the world, the creative integrity of Grand Designs). Mystic Rhythms names the context within which all of this takes place: forces beyond us, worthy of wonder rather than fear.

*Source: [Rush Vault — Mystic Rhythms](https://rushvault.com/2011/02/07/mystic-rhythms/)*

## 6. THE ALBUM'S PHILOSOPHICAL ARCHITECTURE

### Stoicism as the Operating System

Multiple critics and scholars have identified a consistent Stoic philosophical framework running through Neil Peart's lyrics across Rush's catalog, with *Power Windows* being one of its fullest expressions. From [Progarchy's Stoic analysis](http://www.2112.net/powerwindows/transcripts/20140817progarchy.htm):

The Stoic framework operates at four levels relevant to this album:

1. **Seeing clearly** — Territories is fundamentally an exercise in seeing through the fog of territorial narrative to the underlying mechanism ("a piece of dirt," "a change of accent"). The eyeglass in reverse is the Stoic failure: perceiving the world as it appears to your preferences rather than as it is.

2. **What you can and cannot control** — Marathon's "Your meters may overload / You can rest at the side of the road" — you cannot control whether you hit your limit, but you can control the pacing that determines when. Marathon is a Stoic guide to the effort within your power.

3. **Integrity under external pressure** — Grand Designs explicitly maps the Stoic virtue of integrity: "Swimming against the stream / Life in two dimensions is a mass production scheme." The invitation of mass production (conformity) is an invitation to surrender authentic existence.

4. **Time as the master** — Peart's *Marathon* chorus ("From first to last / The peak is never passed... One moment's high, and glory rolls on by / Like a streak of lightning that flashes and fades in the summer sky") is directly Stoic-Marcellian: Marcus Aurelius taught that time sweeps everything away and that this truth, fully absorbed, liberates rather than defeats. The peak always passes *for the person who needs the peak to be permanent*. For the long-game thinker, the passing peak is simply evidence that there are more peaks — and that the run itself is the point.

---

### The Album as a System Map of Power

Each song on *Power Windows* can be read as a different domain where the same structural question is asked: **What is the relationship between the official purpose of this power system and its actual effect on the humans within it?**

| Song | System | Official Purpose | Actual Effect |
|------|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| The Big Money | Capital | Wealth creation | Control, unpredictability, soul extraction |
| Grand Designs | Culture/Art | Quality and meaning | Conformity, image over substance |
| Manhattan Project | Military tech | Security and power | Creation beyond control |
| **Marathon** | **Personal ambition** | **Achievement** | **Possible: sustained growth. Trap: burnout** |
| **Territories** | **Nationalism** | **Identity and protection** | **Division, circular violence, system feeding itself** |
| Middletown Dreams | Social norms | Stability and community | Stasis, suppressed aspiration |
| Emotion Detector | Intimate relationships | Connection | Vulnerability, exposure, the cost of openness |
| Mystic Rhythms | Nature/cosmos | (No purpose — it simply is) | Wonder, humility, the context for everything else |

The album's architecture moves from the largest, most abstract systems (capital, cultural conformity, nuclear technology) through the human-scaled (personal endurance, territorial groups), into the intimate (dreams, emotion) and finally into what transcends all systems (mystic rhythms). It is a complete map of where human beings encounter power — and where power shapes, distorts, sustains, or destroys them.
